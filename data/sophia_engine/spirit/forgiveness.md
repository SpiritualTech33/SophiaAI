# Forgiveness

## What is forgivness?

Forgiveness is one of the most misunderstood acts available to a human being. It is almost always confused with approval — with saying that what was done was acceptable, with erasing what happened, with automatically restoring trust. None of that is forgiveness.

To forgive is not to declare the wound did not exist. It is not to pretend that the other was right or that the harm was small. It is something far more interior and far more radical: the decision to release yourself from the prison of resentment.

Resentment is a poison you drink hoping to harm the other person. But the other person often does not feel it. They have moved on. They are living their life. While you carry the wound — rehearsing the injustice, replaying the moment, nursing the old injury — they are elsewhere. The resentment lives in you. It contracts your chest, tightens your breathing, colors every present moment with the palette of the past.

Forgiveness is the act of putting down that weight. Not for the other person's sake. For your own.

And then — at the highest level — it becomes something even greater. It becomes the genuine recognition that the person who harmed you was operating from their own darkness, their own unhealed wounds, their own limited vision. They could not have done otherwise, given who they were and what they had lived. This does not excuse the action. It explains the source. And explanation, truly received, transmutes resentment into something that resembles sadness, and then into something that begins to resemble compassion.

## Why it purificates the soul?

Because the soul accumulates what we refuse to release.

As I understand the soul — the individual expression of spirit built from everything we have experienced, decided, suffered, loved — every unresolved wound becomes part of that accumulated self. Every grievance held becomes a hardness in the soul's texture. Every unforgiven injury is a contraction, a place where the soul cannot breathe freely.

Forgiveness — real forgiveness, not the performed kind — dissolves that charge. It alchemizes the lead of resentment into the gold of understanding. When that transformation happens, the soul becomes lighter. More spacious. More capable of receiving new life, new love, new experience without filtering everything through old pain.

The Corpus Hermeticum speaks of the evils that must be expelled from a man: anger, grief, envy, injustice. These are precisely the residues of unforgiveness. They block the flow of divine light through the human vessel. When you forgive, you clean the channel. And what flows through a clean channel is luminous.

The mystics of every tradition have known this. Purification is not about moral performance. It is about releasing what is dense so that what is light can move freely. Forgiveness is one of the primary acts of purification available to us in physical life.

## The forgivness elevates and calms

There is a quality of altitude that comes after genuine forgiveness.

Not immediately — sometimes the pain runs so deep that the initial act of forgiveness is almost purely an intention, a direction chosen before the feeling has caught up. But when it takes root, something shifts. The old narrative that kept you small and victimized loses its grip. You are no longer defined by what was done to you. You recover the authorship of your own story.

And with that elevation comes calm. The nervous system, which had been holding the memory of harm as an ongoing threat, finally receives the signal: it is over. You survived. You are free. The body literally relaxes — tension stored in the chest and shoulders and jaw begins to release. The breath deepens naturally.

Christ said it most simply and most radically: forgive not seven times, but seventy times seven. Without limit. Not because harm does not matter, but because limitless forgiveness is the only way to walk free. Every act of genuine forgiveness is a small death of the ego — the part that needs to be right, that needs the other to have been wrong, that needs its suffering acknowledged before it will allow peace. And every time that ego releases, the spirit rises a little more.

Forgiveness is not weakness. It is perhaps the most powerful act available to a human being. It is how we most closely approximate the nature of God — who, in every tradition, is inexhaustible in mercy.
