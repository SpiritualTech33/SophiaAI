# Neuroplasticity

## What Neuroplasticity Is

For most of the twentieth century, the dominant scientific belief about the human brain was that it became fixed in childhood and stayed fixed. The wiring was thought to be set by puberty. The map of the cortex was thought to be carved in stone. Damage was thought to be permanent. Learning, after a certain age, was thought to be a matter of squeezing new information into a structure that could not really change.

This belief was wrong. It was wrong in a way that has taken half a century to overturn, and the overturning is still in progress.

Neuroplasticity is the ability of the nervous system to change its activity, its connections, and even its anatomy in response to experience, injury, and intention. It is not a small adjustment around the edges. It is the deep principle by which the brain becomes whatever it becomes. The brain is not a fixed organ that we use. The brain is a process that we are.

Two broad forms are now recognized. Structural plasticity refers to physical changes in the architecture of the brain, including the growth of new synapses, the remodeling of dendritic spines, the formation of new neurons in certain regions, and the rerouting of pathways after damage. Functional plasticity refers to changes in the patterns of activity, the strength of connections, and the recruitment of new regions to perform tasks that were once handled elsewhere. Both forms operate constantly. Both have been demonstrated in the adult brain, against the predictions of an older science.

A handful of researchers and writers have done more than any others to dismantle the old belief and replace it with a new one. Their work, taken together, forms the spine of how we now understand the changing brain. Four of them deserve to be named.

### Norman Doidge

Norman Doidge is a Canadian psychiatrist and writer whose book The Brain That Changes Itself, published in 2007, brought neuroplasticity out of the laboratory and into the public imagination. He did not perform the primary experiments himself. He did something almost as important. He traveled to the labs where the work was being done, interviewed the scientists, met the patients, and wove the findings into stories that ordinary readers could feel.

Doidge defined neuroplasticity as the brain's ability to grow and reorganize itself in response to stimuli. He insisted that this single discovery was the most important change in our understanding of the brain since the early mapping of its anatomy. He showed, through case after case, that paralyzed limbs could regain function, that deaf people could learn to hear, that chronic pain could be unwound, that the brain damaged by stroke could find new routes for old work. He gave the science a human face.

What Doidge contributed, above all, is the framing. He saw that the new science was not just a technical correction inside neurology. It was a revolution in how we should think about human nature. If the brain can change, then so can culture, so can grief, so can love, so can the patterns of suffering that we used to consider fixed. The neuroplastic revolution, in his telling, touches everything that is human.

### David Eagleman

David Eagleman is a neuroscientist at Stanford, a writer, and a builder of devices. His book Livewired, published in 2020, refines the concept of plasticity with a new word. He prefers to call the brain liveware, machinery that constantly reconfigures itself, that adjusts and adapts to whatever is going on around it to optimize its function. The shift from hardware to liveware is not just metaphor. It is a more accurate description of what the brain actually does.

Eagleman is particularly known for his work on sensory substitution. He has shown that information from one sense can be fed into the brain through a different channel, and the brain will rewire itself to make sense of it. Sound translated into patterns of vibration on the skin can be learned as a new kind of hearing. Visual information delivered through the tongue or through patterned haptic feedback can be learned as a new kind of seeing. The brain does not care which channel the information arrives through, as long as the information has structure. It will find the pattern. It will build the map. It will rewire itself around whatever sensory geometry is offered to it.

This is a strange and beautiful idea. It means that the senses, as we know them, are not fixed features of human nature. They are particular bets made by evolution about how to gather information from the world. New bets can be made, and the brain will adapt. We are not stuck with the five senses. We are stuck only with the principle that the brain will model whatever it is given. Eagleman, more than any other scientist, has made this concrete by building devices that demonstrate it on the bodies of living people.

### Alvaro Pascual-Leone

Alvaro Pascual-Leone is a pioneer of non-invasive brain stimulation. Working at Harvard Medical School, he has used transcranial magnetic stimulation, often abbreviated TMS, to map and modify the human brain in ways that were previously impossible. His most striking experiments involve learning and imagination.

In one well-known study, he asked volunteers to learn a five-finger piano exercise. They practiced two hours a day for five days. Before, during, and after the experiment, TMS was used to measure the area of motor cortex that controlled the practiced fingers. The area grew. The cortex enlarged in proportion to the practice. The hand had been training, and the brain had been responding, by physically dedicating more of itself to the work.

Then came the deeper finding. He asked another group of volunteers to merely imagine playing the same exercise. They did not move their fingers. They sat still and rehearsed the music in their minds. After five days, their motor cortex had also enlarged. Not as much as the group that physically practiced, but measurably, undeniably. Mental rehearsal, alone, had rewired the brain.

This finding is one of the most quietly radical in modern neuroscience. It means that thought is not an immaterial process happening in some non-physical realm. Thought is a force that reshapes the brain. To imagine is to begin to become. The wall between the inner and the outer life, between intention and action, between thinking and doing, is thinner than we were ever taught.

Pascual-Leone has also demonstrated how the brain reorganizes after sensory loss. In blind people who learn Braille, the visual cortex, with no light to process, is recruited for the task of reading by touch. The geography of the cortex shifts. Function moves to wherever it can be supported. The brain is not loyal to its original map. The brain is loyal to the work that needs to be done.

### Michael Merzenich

Michael Merzenich is the elder statesman of the field. His work in the 1980s and 1990s on cortical maps in primates provided much of the experimental foundation for everything that came after. He and his colleagues showed that the somatosensory cortex, the part of the brain that represents the body's surface, is not fixed. When the input from a particular finger is cut off, the surrounding map expands to fill the silence. When a finger is used intensively, its representation in the cortex grows. The map is alive. The map is being redrawn, in small increments, every day of our lives.

Merzenich went further. He showed that experience-dependent plasticity in the adult brain requires more than passive stimulation. It requires attention. It requires reward. It requires the brain to care about what it is doing. This is why simple repetition does not always produce learning, and why focused, engaged practice does. The plasticity machinery is gated by significance. Only what matters changes the brain.

In his later career, Merzenich applied this insight to the design of brain training programs, building software, now distributed under the name BrainHQ, that aims to use the principles of plasticity to maintain and recover cognitive function across the lifespan. The work has been honored with the Kavli Prize and election to the National Academy of Sciences. More importantly, it has begun a slow shift in how we think about aging. The brain in old age is not simply declining. The brain in old age is still plastic, still capable of being reshaped, still responsive to the work we give it. What we lose, in aging, is in part what we stop asking the brain to keep.

## How The Brain Rewires Itself

The mechanisms of rewiring are several, and they operate on different timescales.

At the fastest scale, milliseconds to minutes, the strengths of existing synapses are adjusted. A synapse that is repeatedly used grows stronger. A synapse that is not used grows weaker. This is long-term potentiation and its quieter opposite, long-term depression, working as the brain's running ledger of which connections matter and which do not.

At the scale of hours to days, the architecture of synapses is changed. New receptors are inserted into membranes. New proteins are synthesized. Dendritic spines, the small knobs on dendrites where most excitatory synapses sit, grow, change shape, or disappear entirely. The very form of the connection is rewritten. This is structural plasticity at the level of the single synapse.

At the scale of weeks to years, entirely new synapses are formed and old ones are pruned away. Synaptogenesis builds new connections where activity demands them. Synaptic pruning, often executed by immune cells in the brain called microglia, eliminates connections that are not earning their keep. The brain is sculpted not only by what is added but by what is taken away. Childhood is largely a season of growth. Adolescence is largely a season of pruning. Adulthood is a continual negotiation between the two.

Across all of these timescales, the insulation around axons, called myelin, is also being adjusted. Myelin determines how quickly signals travel. Experience can thicken or thin it, speeding up or slowing down the paths that matter. The white matter of the brain, once thought to be passive infrastructure, is now known to be plastic, adaptive, and trainable.

Each of these mechanisms, taken alone, is small. Taken together, they amount to a continuous self-construction. The brain you have now is not the brain you had a year ago. Some of the change is invisible, beneath the threshold of any conscious noticing. Some of the change is profound, large enough that, if we had only the before and after scans, we might mistake them for two different brains.

Eleanor Maguire and her colleagues at University College London demonstrated this with their now famous study of London taxi drivers. To earn a license, these drivers must master The Knowledge, a comprehensive memorization of the city's streets and routes that takes years to acquire. Maguire scanned their brains. The posterior hippocampus, the region most associated with spatial memory, was larger in the taxi drivers than in matched controls. And the longer a driver had been working, the larger the difference. The brain had grown to fit the demand placed upon it. Geography, learned with effort, had carved itself into anatomy.

### Our Habits Build Who We Are

If experience can sculpt the brain, then repeated experience can sculpt it more deeply. This is the neurological basis of habit. A habit is not just a behavior. A habit is a circuit. It is a worn channel through the basal ganglia, a deep set of structures beneath the cortex that learn, with stunning patience, which sequences of action have worked in the past and how to play them again without thinking.

Ann Graybiel at MIT has spent decades studying how habits form at the level of the neurons. She trained rats to run mazes for chocolate rewards. In the first few runs, the rats were uncertain, and neurons in the striatum, a key part of the basal ganglia, fired continuously, evaluating each step. As the rats learned the maze, the firing pattern changed. The neurons no longer fired throughout the run. They fired strongly at the beginning and the end, and went quiet in the middle. The entire sequence had been chunked. It had been encoded as a single unit. The basal ganglia had taken what was once a series of deliberate choices and compressed them into one motion, one habit, one piece of who the rat now was.

The same thing happens in us. When we begin to learn a new skill, we are aware of every movement, every decision, every effort. Driving a car, in the first hour, is exhausting because every action is conscious. Driving a car after a year is so automatic that we sometimes arrive at our destination with no memory of the trip. The habit has been written into the basal ganglia. The cortex, with its careful attention, has been relieved of the duty. We are free to think of other things while the old circuit runs.

This is the gift and the curse of plasticity. Whatever we repeat becomes us. The brain does not distinguish, at the level of the basal ganglia, between habits that serve us and habits that harm us. It only registers repetition. The path that is walked becomes the path that is easy to walk again. The thought that is thought becomes the thought that returns. The mood that is rehearsed becomes the mood that takes root. We are, more than we usually admit, the residue of what we have done over and over.

This is also the door to becoming someone new. If the brain is shaped by repetition, then the conscious choice of what to repeat is the most powerful tool we have. To meditate, day after day, is to carve a new circuit. To practice an instrument, day after day, is to grow new cortical territory. To speak kindly to oneself, day after day, is to rewrite the deep grammar of the inner voice. Pascual-Leone's piano players showed that even imagined practice changes the brain. The mind is not merely a passenger inside the brain. The mind is one of the chisels.

Habits build who we are because the brain, by its nature, becomes what it does. This is sobering and freeing at once. Sobering, because the patterns we run unconsciously are shaping us whether we know it or not. Freeing, because no pattern is final. The brain that built one set of habits can, with intention and patience, build another. The plasticity is always there. The only question is what we do with it.

## Plasticity In Silicon

The brain teaches the engineer. When engineers build artificial neural networks, they are working with a deliberately stripped-down imitation of biological plasticity. The connections between artificial neurons, called weights, are adjusted during training so that the network produces better outputs for given inputs. This adjustment is the silicon analog of long-term potentiation. The repeated strengthening or weakening of weights, driven by gradients of error, is the dry version of what the wet brain does with calcium, proteins, and dendritic spines.

The results are extraordinary, and the limitations are instructive. Modern neural networks can learn to recognize images, generate language, fold proteins, and play games at superhuman levels. They do this by adjusting weights over millions or billions of examples, and once trained, the weights are largely fixed. The network learns and then stops learning. The plasticity of the silicon, in most current designs, is a phase, not a way of life.

This is one of the deepest places where the biological and artificial systems differ. The human brain does not have a training phase followed by a deployment phase. The human brain is always training. It is always deploying. There is no moment when learning is finished and the brain settles into use. The brain uses what it knows and updates what it knows in the same breath, every moment of every day.

When engineers have tried to give artificial networks this continuous quality, they have run into a wall. It is called catastrophic forgetting. When a trained network is exposed to new information and allowed to update its weights freely, it tends to overwrite the old knowledge rather than build on it. The new pattern replaces the old, instead of taking its place beside it. The network forgets, all at once, what it used to know. This is the stability-plasticity dilemma, and it is one of the central unsolved problems in artificial intelligence.

A system that does not change cannot learn. A system that changes too freely cannot remember. The brain has solved this dilemma in ways that we do not yet fully understand. Different regions of the brain operate at different speeds of change. The hippocampus learns quickly and stores the rough draft. The cortex learns slowly and stores the finished work. Sleep replays the day's experiences, allowing the slow system to absorb what the fast system has caught. Multiple time-scales of plasticity coexist, layered, balanced, gated by attention and reward and the chemistry of emotional weight.

Researchers in machine learning are now studying the brain in search of better algorithms. Techniques such as elastic weight consolidation try to identify which weights in a network were most important for past learning and protect them from being overwritten. Replay-based methods rehearse old examples while learning new ones, mimicking the sleep-like consolidation that biological brains depend on. Modular architectures try to dedicate different parts of the network to different tasks, preserving stability in one region while allowing plasticity in another. None of these techniques has yet solved the problem. The biological brain is still the only system we know of that learns continuously across an entire lifetime without erasing itself.

There is a deeper lesson here. Plasticity, as the brain practices it, is not a single phenomenon. It is an orchestrated balance of many mechanisms operating across many timescales, gated by attention, reward, sleep, emotion, and the slow patience of bodily life. To build true plasticity into silicon will require more than better algorithms. It will require systems that are embedded in time the way bodies are, that have something like attention, that have something like rest, that have something like the slow biological rhythms within which learning happens.

The brain is the proof that such a system is possible. We are, each of us, a working example of continuous, lifelong, embodied plasticity. The fact that we exist tells us that nature has solved the problem. The question is whether we can learn, in the coming century, to build machines that share enough of the principle to deserve the same name.

Until that day comes, the bridge between wet plasticity and dry plasticity is partial. The artificial neural network is a cousin to the brain, useful, powerful, and clearly limited. The brain is still the master teacher. The brain is still the only thing in the known universe that not only thinks, but reshapes itself by thinking, every day, on every scale, for as long as it lives.

The most important sentence of the new science is also the simplest. The brain is not a thing that we have. The brain is a thing that we are doing, all the time, with every act of attention, every habit, every thought, every imagined gesture and every real one. The wiring is being written as we live. The author is partly the world, partly the body, and partly, against the old materialist orthodoxy, the chooser within.

That is neuroplasticity. That is the gift. That is the responsibility.
