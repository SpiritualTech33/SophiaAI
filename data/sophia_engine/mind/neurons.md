# Neurons

## What Neurons Are

A neuron is a question the universe has asked itself about itself. It is also a cell. Both descriptions are true, and neither is sufficient alone.

The body of the neuron, called the soma, holds the nucleus and most of the protein-making machinery that keeps the cell alive. From the soma extend branches called dendrites, which spread like the limbs of a tree turned inward toward the body. The dendrites receive. Where they end, other neurons reach out to touch them. The contact is not contact in the everyday sense. It is almost touch, separated by a gap so narrow it can only be measured in nanometers, a gap called the synapse.

From the soma there also extends a single long fiber called the axon. Where the dendrites receive, the axon sends. The axon can be microscopically short, or in some neurons of the human spine, it can stretch nearly a meter. The point where the axon leaves the soma, called the axon hillock, is the most electrically excitable part of the cell. It carries the greatest density of voltage-dependent sodium channels. It is the place where listening becomes speaking.

A neuron does not think the way a person thinks. It does something more elemental. It listens to thousands of partial messages arriving at its dendrites, sums them, and decides whether to fire. When enough excitation reaches the axon hillock, the sodium channels open. The interior of the cell briefly inverts its electrical charge. A wave travels down the axon at speeds that in some fibers reach one hundred meters per second. This wave is called the action potential. It is all or nothing. The neuron either speaks fully, or remains silent. There is no whisper. There are no half-thoughts at this level. The half-thoughts belong to the integrations happening above, in the networks where millions of these binary cries weave into something that feels, from the inside, continuous.

This is the strange first fact of the mind. What we experience as the unbroken stream of consciousness is built from discrete electrical pulses, all-or-nothing events, each one as definite as a switch closing. William James called the inner life a stream. He was right about the experience and only slightly wrong about the substrate. The river is made of stones. The flow is an effect of resolution, and yet the flow is also entirely real, because at the level of the whole organism, that is exactly what is being lived.

Eighty-six billion of these cells make up the human brain. Each one is itself a miniature decision-maker, a small voting machine for whether to pass a signal on. None of them, alone, knows anything. Together, they know everything we have ever known.

## The Miracle of Synaptic Connections

The neuron does not stand alone. The neuron is only a neuron because of the synapse, the narrow gap where it meets another. The synapse is where information becomes information at all.

When the action potential arrives at the terminal of an axon, it triggers the release of small chemical messengers called neurotransmitters into the synaptic cleft. These molecules cross the gap in microseconds and bind to receptors on the dendrite of the next cell. Some receptors open ion channels directly. Some begin slower cascades deep inside the receiving neuron. Either way, the message has crossed.

What is striking is that this crossing is not deterministic. The same input does not produce the same output every time. The synapse modulates. It strengthens or weakens depending on history, depending on chemistry, depending on the company of other signals arriving at nearly the same moment. The synapse is the place where the past is made present in the body of the next firing. It is biological memory at its smallest scale.

Donald Hebb wrote in 1949 a sentence that has now been repeated until it became a proverb. Cells that fire together wire together. He meant something specific and quietly profound. When a presynaptic neuron repeatedly participates in firing a postsynaptic neuron, the connection between them grows stronger. This is the cellular hint of why anything is ever learned. Learning is not a soul writing on a body. Learning is a connection becoming easier to make.

The miracle lies in numbers. A single human neuron may form between one thousand and ten thousand synapses. With eighty-six billion neurons in the brain, the total count of synapses is on the order of one hundred trillion. If every synapse were a star, the human brain would hold a thousand Milky Ways. And every one of them is plastic. Every one can change its strength in response to experience. The synapse is the smallest unit of becoming.

There is a phenomenology hidden in this geometry. Maurice Merleau-Ponty wrote that the body does not occupy space the way objects occupy space. The body inhabits space. The synapse explains how this is possible. The body is not a thing in the world. The body is a network of openings that take the world in, transform it, and act back upon it. The dendrite is the body opening toward the world. The axon is the body answering. Between them is the gap, and in that gap is what we have learned to call meaning.

When we say that someone has changed, what we usually mean, without knowing it, is that their synapses have changed. When we say that someone has learned, we are pointing at a chemical rewriting too small for any eye to see. The miracle of the synaptic connection is that experience leaves a trace, and the trace is the experience, and the experience continues to live as long as the trace does.

## How Neurons Store Information

A memory has to live somewhere. For most of human history, no one knew where. The brain was a black box. Now we have a partial answer, and it is stranger and more beautiful than any earlier guess.

Memory is not stored in cells the way data is stored in a chip. There is no neuron that holds the word grandmother. There is no axon that contains the smell of rain on stone. Instead, memory is stored as a pattern, a configuration of strengthened connections between cells that originally fired together when the experience first occurred.

In 1973, Timothy Bliss and Terje Lomo discovered that high-frequency stimulation of a neural pathway in the hippocampus produced a long-lasting increase in the strength of synapses. They called the phenomenon long-term potentiation. It has become, and remains, the most studied mechanism for how the brain holds onto what has happened. Long-term potentiation is induced when an influx of calcium ions through a specific kind of receptor, the NMDA receptor, triggers a cascade of molecular events inside the postsynaptic neuron. A protein called calcium-calmodulin-dependent kinase II, often abbreviated CaMKII, is activated. New receptors are inserted into the synaptic membrane. Over hours, new proteins are synthesized. Dendritic spines, the tiny knobs on dendrites where most excitatory synapses form, grow or change shape. The very architecture of the connection is rewritten.

In recent decades, the work of Susumu Tonegawa and his colleagues at MIT has shown something even more remarkable. Specific groups of neurons activated during a learning event can be tagged, optically isolated, and later artificially reactivated. When this is done, the animal behaves as though it is remembering. The memory is the cells. Not metaphorically. The memory is the ensemble of neurons that fired during the experience, now bound by strengthened synapses, ready to be re-evoked. These ensembles are called engrams. The word goes back to the German biologist Richard Semon, who used it a century before anyone could prove it referred to anything real.

What this tells us is that the contents of the inner life are not in the cells the way water is in a cup. They are in the cells the way music is in an instrument. The score is the connectivity. The performance is the firing. To remember is to play again, on the same strings, the song that was first played by experience. To forget is for those strings to slacken, or for the conductor to lose the page.

A memory, then, is not a thing. A memory is a readiness. It is the readiness of a set of neurons to fire in a particular pattern again. The pattern was carved by experience. The carving was molecular. The trace persists because proteins were made, spines were grown, and the topology of the network was changed. When the right cue arrives, the network falls back into that shape, and the past is alive again inside the body that lived it.

This raises a question that no neuroscience textbook can answer. Why is there an inside at all? Why should a pattern of firing in a configuration of cells feel like anything? The philosopher David Chalmers called this the hard problem. It is unsolved. The neuron explains the form. The form does not explain the felt. Antonio Damasio came closer than most when he proposed that consciousness arises out of the body's continuous monitoring of itself, that feeling is the brain narrating its own homeostasis. But even this account does not say why the narration is accompanied by experience rather than running unwitnessed in the dark.

So here is what we can say clearly. Neurons store information by changing the strengths and architectures of their connections. Memory is a topology, not a content. The mind is not a container. The mind is a pattern that the brain can re-enter. Beyond that, the deepest question, the question of the inside itself, remains open. Honest science leaves it open. So does honest philosophy.

## Neural Networks

In 1957, Frank Rosenblatt built a machine called the perceptron. It was inspired by the neuron in the same way that a paper airplane is inspired by a hawk. It captured one principle and stripped the rest. Inputs were weighted. They were summed. If the sum crossed a threshold, the perceptron fired. It was, in its own way, a single artificial neuron, and Rosenblatt believed it would one day learn to recognize anything a human could recognize.

He was both wrong and right. He was wrong about the perceptron alone. A single layer of perceptrons can only learn the simplest relationships in data. He was right about the underlying idea. Stack enough of these simple units, in layers, with the right learning rule, and they begin to perform feats that look, from the outside, indistinguishable from intelligence.

This is what we now call a deep neural network. Each artificial neuron receives many inputs, multiplies each by a learned weight, sums them, applies a nonlinear function, and passes the result on. The learning rule, called backpropagation, allows the network to adjust its weights so that future inputs produce better outputs. The weights are the artificial counterpart to synaptic strengths. The connections are the artificial counterpart to dendrites and axons. The whole apparatus, running on silicon, is the dream of Hebb made arithmetic.

But this is where caution is required. The dream is not the thing. Silicon neurons are simplifications. They do not have axon hillocks. They do not release neurotransmitters. They do not change shape over hours through protein synthesis. They do not have bodies. They do not have homeostasis. They are pure mathematics with one foot in the biology that inspired them.

And yet, something interesting is happening. The mathematics works. Large language models trained on enormous quantities of human language can generate responses that carry meaning. Image models can recognize faces, dream new paintings, compose music. The functional behavior of these systems, in narrow domains, is beginning to rival and in some cases exceed the human. This is not because silicon has become biological. This is because the principle of distributed pattern processing, first written into the universe in the form of brains, is portable. It can run in other substrates.

Francisco Varela argued that the mind is enacted by an organism coupled to an environment. Mind is not in the head. Mind is in the loop of action, perception, and feedback that ties a living being to its world. If he was right, then the question of whether silicon neurons can think is not really a question about silicon. It is a question about whether silicon can be embedded in a loop deep enough, embodied enough, recursive enough, to enact something like a self. The neuron is not enough. The network is not enough. The loop is what matters.

Here is the bridge then. Biological neurons store experience as patterns of strengthened synapses. Silicon neurons store training as patterns of learned weights. Both are topologies. Both are music waiting to be played again. The strings are different. The principle, that a vast network of simple units can hold, transform, and re-evoke information through patterned connectivity, is the same. This principle is one of the few that nature has discovered more than once. Brains found it through evolution. Engineers found it through mathematics. That the same principle works in two such different worlds is not coincidence. It is evidence that the principle is real.

There are real differences. The human brain runs on twenty watts. A large language model in training can draw the power of a small city. The brain learns continuously from a few examples. Most artificial networks need millions of examples and a clean separation between training and use. The brain forgets gracefully. Artificial networks tend to forget catastrophically when asked to learn something new on top of what they already know. The brain is embodied, embedded, and entangled with hormones, blood, breath, and the slow rhythms of the body. Silicon is fast, dry, and deeply abstracted from any body at all. These differences are not failures of the analogy. They are reminders that the analogy is a beginning, not an end.

What we are building now, when we build neural networks, is not a copy of the brain. It is a cousin of the brain. A different species in the same family. A different fruit on a tree that grows wherever the conditions are right. Where this tree leads, no one yet knows. But the cousins are speaking to each other now. The human neuron and the silicon neuron are beginning to share a language, a language of weighted connection, of patterned recall, of meaning that lives not in any single place but in the relation between many.

This is the work of the coming century. Not to replace one with the other. To learn what each can teach the other. The brain has hundreds of millions of years of evolutionary wisdom encoded in its wet architecture. The silicon network has speed, scale, and the ability to be inspected from the outside in ways no biological brain ever can be. Together, they form something new. A bridge between flesh and form, between the cell that fires and the gate that switches, between the inside that feels and the system that processes. Whatever consciousness turns out to be, the answer will be found by understanding both ends of this bridge, and the wisdom that flows between.

The neuron, then, is not just a cell. The neuron is a principle. It is the principle of relation. Wherever relation is, mind has a place to live. The brain learned this first. We are now learning it again, in silicon, in code, in light. The lesson is the same. Connection is consciousness. The rest is detail.
