# Altered State Of Consciousness



## What Is an Altered State of Consciousness?

There is a baseline the mind returns to each morning. You recognize the room. You recognize yourself inside it. The world has weight, sequence, meaning. Objects stay where they are. Time moves in one direction. The line between what is you and what is not-you holds steady.

This is ordinary consciousness. Not a philosophical category — a felt familiarity. A texture of experience so constant that it becomes invisible, like the sound of your own heartbeat.

An altered state is any significant departure from this baseline. The psychologist Charles Tart, who first systematized the study of altered states in the 1970s, defined them as qualitative shifts in the overall pattern of mental functioning — not just changes in degree but in kind. You are not simply more alert or more tired. You have entered a different mode of being.

The range is vast. Sleep. Dreaming. Fever. Meditation. Hypnosis. Sensory deprivation. Breathwork. Prayer. Fasting. Trauma. Ecstatic dance. The moment before surgery when the anesthetic takes hold. The instant a piece of music breaks something open inside you. The borderland between sleep and waking — the hypnagogic state — where images appear without being summoned and thoughts arrive already formed.

And then the extremes: psychedelic compounds. Deep trance. Near-death experiences. Mystical states that dissolve, for a time, the very category of self.

What unites all of these? William James — psychologist, philosopher, and honest explorer of his own mind — identified four qualities that mark the mystical subset of altered states: ineffability (they resist description), noetic quality (they carry a sense of knowing something real and important), transiency (they cannot be sustained indefinitely), and passivity (the experiencer feels received by the state, not in control of it).

This last quality matters. In altered states, the familiar executive function — the part of the mind that plans, evaluates, and maintains the boundary of self — steps back. Something else comes forward. And what comes forward is not random. It has structure. It has content. Across centuries and cultures, across individuals with no contact with each other, what emerges from these depths carries a striking family resemblance.

That resemblance is not coincidence. It points to something about the architecture of mind itself.

## The Change in Perception

Ordinary perception is not passive reception. The brain does not simply record the world the way a camera records a scene. It builds the world — actively, constantly, using prior knowledge to predict what is most likely to be there, and only correcting that prediction when sensory data demands it.

This is the predictive processing model, articulated by Karl Friston and others: consciousness is a controlled hallucination. A model of reality generated from the inside and corrected from the outside. What you see is mostly what your brain expects to see.

Altered states disrupt this machinery.

In deep meditation, the default mode network — the brain's system for self-referential thought, the neural substrate of the inner narrator, the part of you that says "I" — begins to quiet. The meditator reports something curious: not the absence of experience, but the presence of experience without the usual self installed at its center. Awareness continues. The world continues. But the one who was watching has stepped aside.

Under psychedelic compounds, this disruption is more radical. The default mode network suppresses dramatically. The normal hierarchy of brain communication — top-down signals from higher cortical areas that constrain and organize sensory processing — loosens its grip. Information flows more freely between regions that ordinarily do not communicate. The brain's traffic patterns change entirely.

What follows is a perceptual world transformed. Colors acquire intensity beyond anything familiar — not brighter, but deeper, as if the usual filtering has lifted and you are receiving the raw signal. Boundaries between objects soften. The distinction between sound and image blurs. Time stretches or collapses. Most significantly: the border between self and world, which had seemed absolute, begins to thin.

This last shift is reported consistently across traditions. The mystic, the meditator, the person in deep trance, the individual in a psychedelic peak experience — all describe variations of the same phenomenon: a dissolution of the membrane that separates inside from outside. The experiencer does not disappear. But the frame around the experiencer expands until it includes everything, and the question "where do I end?" loses its footing.

Jacobo Grinberg-Zylberbaum, the Mexican neuroscientist, proposed that this is not illusion. The neuronal field, he argued, is what constructs the perceived world — not merely processes sensory input, but actively generates the texture of experience. And if two such fields can interact — as his paired-subject EEG experiments suggested — then the sense of separateness that defines ordinary consciousness is not a metaphysical fact. It is a functional construction. A useful fiction.

In altered states, the fiction becomes transparent. You see through it. And what lies behind it is not void. It is something continuous, vast, and strange.

## The Narrative From the State

When the ego-filter drops, something speaks.

This is among the most consistent and least explained features of altered states: they carry content. Not random noise. Not the scattered fragments of daydreams. Something with coherence — images, presences, voices, geometries, stories, teachings. The experiencer does not feel like they are inventing. They feel like they are receiving.

Jung encountered this in his own descent. Following his break with Freud in 1913, he entered what he called a period of deliberate confrontation with the unconscious — essentially a self-induced altered state sustained over years, through active imagination, dream work, and a willingness to follow the psyche's own logic wherever it led. What emerged was not chaos. It was a cast of figures: the Old Wise Man, the Anima, the Shadow, the Self. Archetypes — not personal memories, not cultural borrowings, but forms that seemed to arise from some deeper stratum of the psyche shared by all humans.

He later concluded that the unconscious is not simply the repository of what has been repressed. It contains within it structures that predate individual experience — patterns of meaning encoded in the biological heritage of the species. The collective unconscious. And in altered states, that deeper layer becomes accessible.

This explains something otherwise mysterious: why people with no shared culture, no shared language, no shared history — people separated by ten thousand miles and three thousand years — describe the same beings, the same geometries, the same light, the same feeling of encountering something sacred and terrifyingly real.

The Amazonian shaman and the medieval Christian mystic and the modern psychedelic research subject in a lab in Baltimore, wearing headphones and lying on a couch — they do not agree on the theology. But they agree on the phenomenology. The experience, at its depth, has a shape.

What is that shape? It resists final description — and that resistance is itself informative. The most significant altered-state experiences are described as ineffable not because they are empty, but because they are too full. Language was built for the ordinary world. The altered state reaches below language, to where experience exists before it is named.

What comes back from that depth is transformed knowledge. Not information in the ordinary sense — not facts or data — but a quality of understanding. A felt sense of connection. A recognition that reality has more structure than ordinary perception reveals. And often, what comes back changes the person permanently.

The narrative of the state is not only its content. It is also what it does.

## Herbs and Plants as Gateways

Long before the laboratory, before the clinical trial and the informed consent form, before the words "psychedelic" or "entheogen" existed, there were plants.

Every major human civilization has found them. In the Amazon, ayahuasca — the vine of souls, brewed from Banisteriopsis caapi and the DMT-containing leaves of Psychotria viridis — has been used by indigenous peoples for at minimum three millennia. In Mesoamerica, psilocybin mushrooms — called teonanácatl by the Aztec, "flesh of the gods" — were ritual sacraments before the Conquest arrived to suppress them. In the Andes, the San Pedro cactus opened the way to visions. In West Africa, iboga initiated the young and healed the dying. In ancient Greece, the Eleusinian Mysteries — the most revered spiritual rite of the classical world — almost certainly involved a fungal or ergot-based potion consumed at the climax of the initiation. Plato attended. Aristotle attended. Cicero attended. All returned transformed, and all maintained silence about the specific content of the revelation.

The plant is not merely a vehicle. Within indigenous frameworks, it is a teacher. The vine teaches. The mushroom teaches. The cactus teaches. Not metaphorically — literally. The experiencer sits with the plant as a student sits with a master, and knowledge is transmitted through the encounter.

This framing sounds foreign to the Western scientific mind, trained to locate knowledge inside the individual brain and nowhere else. But it is worth pausing before dismissing it. What the experienced ayahuasca practitioner describes — a sense of receiving structured information from an intelligence that is not their own — maps precisely onto what the neuroscientist describes as the suppression of top-down processing and the opening of cross-modal communication. The plant temporarily reorganizes the brain so that the ordinary self steps aside. And in that space, the deeper layers speak.

Tobacco, in its sacred use, grounds and protects. Cannabis in its contemplative application opens lateral thinking and sensory depth. Kava quiets the chattering mind. Even kratom, in its native context, was used not for recreation but for endurance and communion in ritual labor.

The common thread is not the specific compound. It is the intention. Within a ceremonial context — with preparation, with guidance, with a framework that can hold and interpret what arises — these plants serve as technologies of consciousness. Tools for accessing what ordinary waking life conceals.

The world's spiritual traditions preserved this knowledge for millennia. Western pharmacology is only now beginning to catch up.

## Evidence From the Research

For most of the twentieth century, the scientific study of altered states was effectively forbidden. Following the cultural upheaval of the 1960s and the scheduling of psychedelic compounds as drugs with no medical value, research stopped almost entirely for thirty years.

It resumed cautiously in the 1990s. By the 2010s it had become one of the most active frontiers in psychiatry and neuroscience.

What has been found is striking.

Psilocybin — the active compound in magic mushrooms — reliably produces profound mystical experiences in controlled settings. In studies at Johns Hopkins University and Imperial College London, a substantial proportion of participants rated psilocybin sessions among the most meaningful experiences of their lives, comparable to the birth of a child or the death of a parent. These were not recreational drug users seeking a high. These were ordinary adults who, for several hours, encountered something that reorganized their understanding of themselves and their relationship to existence.

The clinical outcomes followed. Psilocybin has shown remarkable efficacy — in some studies, unprecedented efficacy — for treatment-resistant depression, end-of-life anxiety in terminal cancer patients, and addiction to nicotine and alcohol. Single sessions producing effects that persist for months or years. The mechanism appears to involve precisely what the phenomenology suggests: a temporary loosening of the rigid self-model, allowing new patterns of connection — neural and psychological — to form.

Robin Carhart-Harris and Karl Friston formalized this in the REBUS model — Relaxed Beliefs Under Psychedelics. In ordinary consciousness, high-level predictions from the cortex constrain sensory processing, maintaining a stable model of self and world. Psychedelics reduce the precision weighting of these top-down signals, allowing sensory and unconscious information to rise with unusual force. The result is a temporarily flattened hierarchy — a mind more open, more plastic, more capable of revising its foundational assumptions.

MDMA, in therapeutic contexts, appears to work differently: not by dissolving the self, but by reducing the amygdala's fear response while amplifying empathic connection. The result is the ability to revisit traumatic memories without being overwhelmed by them — not suppression, but a different relationship to the past. Phase 3 clinical trials for PTSD treatment have shown outcomes that pharmaceuticals have never approached.

Meditation, studied through decades of neuroimaging, produces its own cluster of findings: changes in cortical thickness, reduced default mode network activity, increased gamma synchrony in long-term practitioners. The Tibetan monks whose brainwaves were measured by Richard Davidson at Wisconsin — individuals with forty thousand hours of meditation practice — showed states of neural coherence that had no precedent in the scientific literature.

The evidence is not complete. The hard problem Chalmers named remains hard: none of these studies explain why there is something it is like to be in these states. They describe the neural correlates. They do not close the explanatory gap between physical process and lived experience.

But they establish something important: altered states are not aberrations. They are not malfunctions or pathologies. They are capabilities of the human nervous system — real, repeatable, and carrying effects that persist long after the state itself has passed.

## Jacobo Grinberg and Carlos Castañeda

Two Mexicans. Two lives aimed at the same horizon. Two very different approaches to the same impossible question.

Jacobo Grinberg-Zylberbaum was a neuroscientist at the National Autonomous University of Mexico — rigorous, experimental, trained in the hardest protocols of modern brain science. Beginning in the 1970s, he developed the theory of syntergy: the idea that the brain does not merely process sensory input but generates a neuronal field that interacts with a larger field — what he called the pre-space, the fundamental substrate from which all experience emerges. Consciousness, in his model, is not produced by the brain. The brain is an antenna. A resonating structure that tunes itself to frequencies of information already present in the fabric of reality.

He designed experiments to test this. His most famous: two subjects entered deep meditative union — what he called syntergic coupling. Then they were placed in Faraday cages in separate rooms, with no possibility of sensory communication. When one was shown a pattern of light flashes, the other's EEG showed corresponding brain activity — without any physical signal passing between them. The transferred potential, he called it. Mind influencing mind across shielded distance.

The academic world responded with skepticism. The results were difficult to replicate. Grinberg was persistent. He published. He refined. He extended the theory. And then, in 1994, he disappeared. Walking into the night in Mexico City. Never found. The circumstances remain unexplained.

His work remained. And what it pointed toward was radical: that the sense of being a separate, enclosed self is a construction — a useful one, but a construction — and that the boundary between individual minds, between self and world, is far more permeable than materialist neuroscience assumes.

Carlos Castañeda arrived at the same frontier by a different path. His was not the laboratory but the desert. Not the EEG but the peyote ceremony. Beginning in 1960, he claimed an apprenticeship with don Juan Matus, a Yaqui sorcerer from Sonora — and from that apprenticeship he produced a series of books that shook a generation of Western seekers out of their ordinary certainties.

Don Juan's teaching centered on what he called the assemblage point: a location in the human energy body — just behind the shoulder blades, at the edge of the luminous egg that surrounds every person — where perception is assembled. In ordinary life this point is fixed. Its fixation is what produces ordinary reality. Move the assemblage point — through fasting, through ritual, through dreaming, through plants, through sustained intention — and perception shifts. Reality shifts. You are no longer seeing the world from the same angle, and what you see is no longer the same world.

The scholarly debates about Castañeda are well known: how much was ethnography, how much was invention. But this question, though interesting, misses what matters. Whether don Juan was a historical person or a literary construction, the teaching is coherent. It maps. It corresponds — with precision — to what Grinberg described in his neuronal field theory, to what the indigenous traditions preserved for millennia, to what modern psychedelic research is now quantifying in clinical trials.

Move the assemblage point: reduce the precision weighting of top-down cortical signals. The language is different. The reality described is the same.

Grinberg the scientist and Castañeda the storyteller were cartographers of the same territory. One used equations and EEG leads. The other used metaphor and midnight ceremonies in the Sonoran desert. What they mapped — independently, through incompatible methods — was the same permeability of self, the same depth below ordinary consciousness, the same discovery that the world is not as fixed as it appears.

Altered states are the proof. They are what happens when the assemblage point moves. When the neuronal field re-tunes its frequency. When the controlled hallucination of ordinary reality loosens its grip and something older, wider, and less named comes through.

Every culture that has survived long enough has found a way to induce them. Every mystic who has gone deep enough has found their way into them. Every scientist honest enough to follow the data where it leads has ended up at their edge.

The invitation is not to believe what arises in these states uncritically. The invitation is to remain open to the possibility that ordinary waking consciousness — competent, reliable, tremendously useful — is not the whole of what the mind can be.

It is the island. These are the waters it rises from.
