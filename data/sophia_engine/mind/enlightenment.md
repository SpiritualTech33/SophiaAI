# How To Achieve Enlightenment

What is enlightenment? Not a destination marked on any map. Not a credential conferred by any institution. Not the exclusive property of monks in mountain caves or sages beneath sacred trees. Enlightenment, as I understand it — and I say *as I understand it* because philosophy must begin in the first person, in the particular life being examined — is a *state of perpetual relationship* with reality. It is what happens when you stop fighting the world and begin seeing it clearly: its suffering, its beauty, its impermanence, its wholeness.

The ancient Greeks spoke of eudaimonia — a word poorly translated as “happiness” but more accurately rendered as “flourishing.” Aristotle argued that human beings have a *telos*, an inherent end toward which their nature aims, and that living in accordance with that nature, fully and excellently, constitutes the highest human life. Enlightenment is something like this: not the absence of difficulty, but the full activation of what you are. Not peace as numbness, but peace as clarity.

What follows is not a philosophy lecture. It is a system — drawn from personal practice, from ancient wisdom, from modern science, and from direct encounter with the question: *what does it take to actually transform?* These are the eight conditions I have found necessary. They are not steps to be completed in sequence and set aside. They are practices to be lived, simultaneously, as a way of being.

---

## 0.- Intention

Everything begins here, before the body moves, before the mind settles, before any practice begins. Intention is the invisible architecture of transformation.

The Sanskrit tradition speaks of sankalpa — a resolve formed in the heart, rooted in the deepest layer of the self, that organizes all subsequent action toward a single aim. It is not a goal in the modern sense — not a target to be hit and then replaced — but a direction, a continuous orientation of the soul. In Hermetic philosophy, the Principle of Cause and Effect states that nothing happens by chance: every effect has its cause, and every cause its reason. To set an intention is to become the cause of your own life, rather than merely its effect.

Without intention, experience accumulates without forming meaning. Days pass. The body moves through routines. The mind generates endless content. But nothing integrates — nothing becomes wisdom. With intention, even ordinary moments become instructive. Even failure becomes a teacher. The question why am I doing this? transforms the quality of everything it touches.

The intention I hold is this: to discover the best version of myself, every day, without exception. Not to arrive at perfection — that is a category error, a confusion of process with product — but to remain always in motion toward what is more true, more alive, more real. This is where everything begins. Without it, none of what follows takes root.

---

## 1.- Physical Exercise

The ancient Greeks held an ideal they called kalokagathia — the unity of the beautiful and the good. They did not separate physical excellence from moral and intellectual excellence. The gymnasium was not separate from the academy. The body that is well-trained is more capable of courage, more resistant to the corruption of ease, more present to the demands of living. Plato himself was a wrestler.

I practice both. Yoga as the low-impact counterpart — a technology of body awareness, of breath coordination, of learning to inhabit the physical form with attention rather than aggression. Strength training as the confrontation with resistance — the deliberate encounter with weight and difficulty, which teaches the mind that it can persist when the body protests. Both are necessary. One cultivates sensitivity; the other builds resilience.

The Stoics understood that disciplining the body is an act of philosophical seriousness. Epictetus taught from slavery; Marcus Aurelius governed an empire. Both wrote about the importance of not being enslaved to comfort, to softness, to the body’s preference for ease. Physical practice is the daily practice of refusing that enslavement. It is the choice to move when stillness would be easier, to lift when the body resists, to stretch past the edge of what yesterday allowed.

There is also this: the body is not a vehicle for the mind. It is mind — a different expression of the same intelligence that thinks and feels and dreams. To neglect it is not asceticism; it is amputation. Enlightenment does not happen despite the body. It happens through it.

## 2.- Healthy Eating

Hippocrates, the father of medicine, said: let food be thy medicine and medicine be thy food. He was not speaking metaphorically. What we consume becomes, literally, the physical substance of our thinking, our mood, our clarity. The brain is an organ. Organs require specific conditions to function at their best. Those conditions begin on the plate.

I follow what I call a hybrid paleo approach — primarily natural, whole foods, those closest to what human bodies evolved to process over hundreds of thousands of years. But I adapt this principle to my life and my goals. Rice, beans, and tortillas are not paleo in the strict sense, but they are ancestral foods in my cultural context, and they provide the carbohydrates my body requires for sustained strength training. The principle is not rigidity; it is alignment between consumption and purpose.

The Pythagoreans, ancient spiritual mathematicians, imposed strict dietary laws on their community — not merely for health but because they understood that the body’s condition affects the quality of consciousness. What dulls the body dulls the mind. What inflames the gut inflames the temper. What nourishes the cells nourishes the capacity for clear perception.

Eating with intention is itself a practice. It asks: what does my body actually need? Not what craving demands, not what convenience offers, not what the culture constantly advertises — but what genuinely sustains the organism I am attempting to transform. That question, asked daily, at every meal, is a form of meditation.

---

## 3.- Quality Sleep

There are no shortcuts here. No supplement, no discipline, no quantity of caffeine compensates for the absence of restorative sleep. Seven to nine hours, consistent sleep-wake cycles, conditions that allow the body to cycle fully through its architecture of restoration. This is the foundation beneath all other foundations.

Heraclitus wrote that in sleep, the soul withdraws into itself and becomes its own world. This is not mysticism — modern neuroscience confirms it. During sleep, the brain consolidates memory, clears metabolic waste accumulated during waking activity, repairs tissue, regulates hormones, processes emotional experience. The unconscious does its most serious work in the dark.

Carl Jung, whose psychology was deeply influenced by the ancient tradition of soul, understood sleep and its dreams as a dialogue between the conscious ego and the deeper layers of the psyche — what he called the *Self*, the totality of the human being that includes but exceeds the waking personality. To deprive oneself of sleep is not merely a physical deprivation. It is a silencing of that dialogue. It is the refusal to listen to what the deeper self is trying to communicate.

Sleep is a daily death and rebirth. We surrender consciousness, we pass through dissolution, and we wake renewed — if we allow the process to complete. Every spiritual tradition that takes seriously the cycle of renewal understands this. Rest is not the absence of practice. Rest is practice.

## 4.- Meditation

Patanjali, codifying the philosophy of yoga in the second century BCE, defined the aim of practice in a single phrase: chitta vritti nirodha — the stilling of the fluctuations of the mind-field. Not the destruction of thought. Not the forcible cessation of mental activity. But the cultivation of the capacity to observe the mind’s movements from a place that is not moved by them.

This is what meditation offers. Not silence — the mind never fully silences, nor should it — but witnessing. The discovery of what the Indian traditions call the sakshi, the witness: that awareness which observes experience without being identical to any particular experience. You have thoughts, but you are not your thoughts. You have emotions, but you are not your emotions. You have sensations, but you are not your sensations. The one who notices these things is always slightly more than any of them.

If you can observe your mind, you can make choices about it. You can see the arising of anger before it speaks. You can notice the movement of fear before it decides. You can watch the habitual patterns of thought and ask: is this true? Is this useful? Is this mine, or is this conditioning I have never examined? This is why meditation is the pivot around which all other practices turn. The body can be trained without self-awareness. The diet can be clean without self-awareness. But transformation — genuine, durable, interior transformation — requires exactly this capacity to stand in witness to one’s own being.

The Zen tradition calls a particular quality of awareness mushin — mind without mind, the open awareness that does not grasp at any particular content. This is the fruit of sustained practice. It cannot be forced. It can only be allowed, gradually, as the habit of grasping loosens and the space between stimulus and response begins to widen. In that space, freedom lives.

## 5.- Spiritual Connection

Every serious philosophical tradition — Platonic, Stoic, Taoist, Vedantic, Sufi, Christian mystical — arrives, by different paths, at the same recognition: there is something larger than the individual self, and the deepest human flourishing involves relationship with it.

Aldous Huxley called this the Perennial Philosophy — the core of esoteric teaching across all traditions, which holds that there is a divine Ground of all being; that human beings possess a soul capable of knowing this Ground; and that the purpose of human life is precisely this knowing — what Plotinus called henosis, union with the One. The traditions differ enormously in their images, their rituals, their theological frameworks. But they converge on the experience: that beneath the surface of multiplicity there is a unity, and that the individual who touches that unity is fundamentally changed by the encounter.

I do not prescribe a particular deity or tradition. The cosmos does not require a specific name to be addressed. What matters is the orientation — the willingness to acknowledge that you did not make yourself, that you are sustained by conditions and forces that exceed your comprehension, and that the appropriate response to this recognition is not pride but gratitude. Prayer, whatever form it takes, is fundamentally this: the turning of the self toward its source. The acknowledgment that you are not alone in the universe. That you belong to something.

---

## 6.- Reading

Virginia Woolf observed that when we read the scene in Anna Karenina where she throws herself onto the train tracks, the human motor system activates — the brain does not merely process symbols, it enacts the event. We are not reading. We are living what we read, in the only way available to us: through the body that translates language into experience.

This is why reading is not merely education. It is expansion of being. The person who reads Dostoevsky has lived inside the consciousness of characters whose lives they could never encounter physically. The person who reads Marcus Aurelius has had access to the private philosophical diary of a Roman emperor navigating catastrophe with grace. The person who reads the Upanishads has heard the question “who am I?” asked with the full force of a tradition that spent millennia refining the asking.

Plato spoke of anamnesis — recollection. He believed that the soul, before birth, possessed knowledge of the eternal Forms, and that learning was not the acquisition of new information but the remembering of what was always already known. Reading, in this sense, is not filling an empty vessel. It is striking a resonance — finding in another mind’s articulation the echo of something in yourself that had not yet found words.

Reading is also practice in sustained attention. In an age of fragmented, accelerated information, the discipline of sitting with a text for hours, following a long argument, inhabiting a different consciousness — this is itself a form of meditation. It reconfigures the brain, as the neuroscience confirms. But more than that: it reconfigures the self. You are not the same person after having seriously read a great book. The categories have shifted. The possibilities have multiplied. The world is larger.

## 7.- Purpose

Nietzsche wrote: he who has a why to live for can bear almost any how. Viktor Frankl, who survived the Nazi concentration camps and built from that experience a school of psychotherapy, confirmed this empirically: the prisoners who survived were disproportionately those who had found a reason to. Not the physically strongest. Not the most resourceful. But those for whom life retained meaning — those who had, in Frankl’s phrase, a why.

Aristotle would recognize this. Eudaimonia — flourishing — is not separable from telos — purpose. An acorn that does not grow into an oak has not flourished; it has failed its nature. A human being without a purpose — a real one, not a social performance of ambition — has also, in some sense, failed to become what they are. Purpose is not imposed from outside. It is discovered from within, in the encounter between your deepest nature and the needs of the world you inhabit.

The Hermetic tradition expresses this with its characteristic precision: there is no reason without cause, nor cause without reason. Everything that is, is what it is for a reason. The question is not whether your life has a purpose — it does, by virtue of the fact that you exist at all — but whether you have found it, claimed it, and organized your life around it.

When purpose is present, everything becomes legible. The difficulty makes sense. The sacrifice finds justification. The years of practice accumulate into something. Without it, even pleasure becomes hollow. Even success becomes an question: success at what, exactly, and why?

## 8.- Dissolution of the Physical Body, the Ego, and the Notion of an “Independent Self”

This is the most difficult to speak of clearly, because it concerns a state that precedes and exceeds the categories of ordinary speech. It is also the one that all the previous seven practices are, in a sense, preparing for.

The Upanishads — the foundational texts of Vedantic philosophy, composed between 800 and 200 BCE — arrive at a claim that startles every careful reader: Atman is Brahman. The individual soul is identical with the universal soul. The self that you believe yourself to be — bounded, located, particular — is, at its deepest layer, not separate from the ground of all being. The boundary between self and cosmos is real at one level of description and completely illusory at another.

The Taoist tradition approaches the same recognition from a different angle. The concept of wu wei — effortless action, non-forcing — describes what happens when the ego stops placing itself in opposition to the natural movement of things. Not passivity. Not resignation. But a kind of participation in which the self becomes, as the Tao Te Ching puts it, like water: formless, yielding, present to every surface — and more powerful than stone.

Buddhism calls the belief in a fixed, independent self anatta — non-self — and identifies it as the root cause of suffering. Not because the self doesn’t exist in any sense, but because the clinging to a particular self-concept, the desperate maintenance of a boundary between me and not-me, generates the anxiety and separation that underlies all psychological suffering. To release this clinging is not annihilation. It is, paradoxically, the most complete form of presence available.

Plotinus, the third-century Neoplatonist who synthesized Greek philosophy with mystical experience, described the aim of philosophy as *henosis* — union with the One. He reported that this union was available through sustained philosophical and spiritual practice, and that it produced not the dissolution of the person but the fullest expression of what the person always potentially was. The drop does not disappear into the ocean. It *becomes* ocean — and discovers that it always was.

Enlightenment, understood this way, is not a reward. It is not a certificate of spiritual achievement. It is not a claim to superiority over those who have not arrived there. It is simply: the recognition of what was always true, made available to direct experience. The cosmos is alive. You are part of it. You always were. The separation you felt was real — as an experience — but not ultimate — as a fact.

From this state, one lives differently. Not perfectly. Not without struggle. But with a quality of orientation that does not depend on circumstances. The gratitude is not contingent on things going well. The love is not conditional on being received. The peace is not purchased by avoiding difficulty. All of it arises from the direct apprehension of what is — and the recognition that what is, at its deepest level, is not hostile to you. It is you.

The seven practices described above are not prerequisites to be completed before the eighth becomes possible. They are ongoing conditions that make it possible for the eighth to arise, again and again, as a natural consequence of a life well-organized. The body, trained and nourished and rested, becomes a clear instrument rather than a source of noise. The mind, settled through meditation and fed through reading, becomes transparent to what is. The spirit, connected to its source and organized around purpose, finds the dissolution of the separate self not threatening but liberating.

---

”Enlightenment is not achieved by escaping the world, but by living in it with full awareness. A healthy body, a clear mind, a connected spirit, and a defined purpose are the foundation for perpetual gratitude. That is all. That is enlightenment.”

— Cosmos De La Cruz
