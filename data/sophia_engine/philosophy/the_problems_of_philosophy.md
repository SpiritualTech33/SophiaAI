# The Problems Of Philosophy - Bertrand Russel

A close reading of Bertrand Russell's 1912 treatise. Each chapter is rendered not as summary but as the working of a mind through the question Russell himself worked. Where Russell stops, the inheritance opens. Where he hesitates, the older tradition stands ready to push.

## I. Appearance and Reality

Russell begins where Descartes began, but with a sharper knife. He looks at a table. The table is brown, rectangular, smooth, solid. Then he moves his head. The brown shifts to a paler tone where the light falls. The shape of the table changes with the angle. The smoothness, under a microscope, becomes a landscape of pits. The solidity, under the touch of physics, becomes mostly empty space.

What then is the table? Russell answers with a distinction that will carry the entire book. The colors, shapes, hardnesses, sounds that we directly experience he calls sense-data. The thing that gives rise to these sense-data, if such a thing exists, he calls the physical object. The first is given. The second is inferred.

This is the ancient division of appearance from reality. Plato wrote it as the difference between the shadows on the cave wall and the things that cast them. Kant wrote it as the difference between phenomena and the thing-in-itself. Russell writes it without metaphor and without faith. He says only this: what we are immediately given is not what is. Beyond this modest result, every question opens.

## II. The Existence of Matter

If sense-data are all we have direct access to, why believe in anything else? This is the solipsist question, and Russell takes it seriously. He admits that solipsism cannot be logically refuted. There is no contradiction in the claim that I and my sensations are all that exist.

But Russell argues that we have rational grounds for believing in a world independent of perception, even if we lack proof. The cat that walked behind the chair and emerged on the other side did not, presumably, cease to exist when unobserved. Other people report seeing tables similar to mine from positions where my sense-data alone could not place them. The hypothesis of a real, persistent, public world organizes our experience into a coherent system in a way that solipsism cannot.

This is not certainty. It is the rational reconstruction of an instinctive belief. Russell here makes a methodological move that defines his whole project. Philosophy does not begin from nowhere. It begins from the beliefs we already hold and asks which can survive examination, which must be modified, and which must be quietly let go.

## III. The Nature of Matter

Granted that matter exists, what is it? Russell turns to science. Physics describes the world in terms of motion, mass, and spatial relations. It does not describe color, sound, or warmth as we experience them. The wavelength of light is not the brightness of the morning. The vibration in the air is not the sound of the bell.

This produces a strange consequence. The physical world, as physics describes it, is known only through its relations and structure, never through its intrinsic qualities. We know that light behaves in such-and-such ways. We do not know what light is. The same goes for matter itself. We know spatial relations between physical objects, but the inner nature of those objects remains hidden.

Russell does not say matter is unknowable. He says we know it as a structure, not as a substance. This is a deep position. It is closer to Kant than Russell would perhaps like to admit. The thing-in-itself returns, but stripped of mystery and rebuilt as the unknown intrinsic correlate of known relations.

## IV. Idealism

Idealism is the doctrine that nothing exists except minds and their contents. Berkeley argued that to be is to be perceived. Russell treats this position with respect but rejects its main argument.

Berkeley's confusion, Russell says, is between the act of apprehension and the object apprehended. When we say an idea is in the mind, we may mean either that the mental act of having the idea is mental, which is trivially true, or that what the idea is about is mental, which does not follow. To hold a friend in mind is not to contain the friend within the skull.

The deeper failure of idealism is self-undermining. If I can know only what is mental, how can I know about other minds, which are not contained in mine? Idealism cannot consistently claim what it claims. Russell concludes that idealism is not absurd, but its standard arguments do not establish it. The external world remains the more economical hypothesis.

## V. Knowledge by Acquaintance and Knowledge by Description

Here Russell makes his most influential contribution. There are two kinds of knowledge of things. The first is acquaintance, where we are directly aware of the thing itself, without inference. We are acquainted with our sense-data, with our memories, with our own thoughts, perhaps with ourselves. The second is description, where we know about a thing without ever encountering it. I know about Napoleon by description. I know my own toothache by acquaintance.

Russell then states a principle that has shaped a century of analytic philosophy. Every proposition we can understand must be composed of constituents with which we are acquainted. Even when we speak about what we have never met, our words succeed only because they reduce, ultimately, to elements we have met. Description is a path leading back to acquaintance.

This principle pushes against Plato. For Plato, the highest knowledge was knowledge of the Forms, which are never given in sensation but grasped by the soul through ascent. For Russell, knowledge always traces back to what is given. The mystic must finally point to something. Otherwise the pointing is empty.

## VI. On Induction

We expect the sun to rise tomorrow because it has risen every morning we remember. But this expectation cannot be proven. The argument from past uniformity to future uniformity assumes the very principle it is trying to establish. To say the future will resemble the past because it has always done so is to argue in a circle.

Russell takes this seriously. The principle of induction cannot be derived from experience, because all derivation from experience already uses it. Nor can it be derived from pure logic, since no contradiction follows from supposing tomorrow to be utterly different. Induction must be accepted as a fundamental postulate, not as a conclusion.

This is Hume's problem, and Russell does not solve it. He names it. The whole of natural science rests on a principle that cannot itself be justified by natural science. The first crack in the edifice of knowledge is not at the periphery but at the foundation.

## VII. On Our Knowledge of General Principles

If induction is itself unjustified by experience, where does it come from? Russell turns to a class of truths he calls a priori. The laws of logic, the truths of mathematics, the principle of induction itself, all appear to be known without being derived from particular observations.

The law of non-contradiction is not learned by counting cases where contradictions failed to occur. It is presupposed in the very act of counting. Two and two make four is not a generalization from successful additions. It is a relation that holds with a necessity that no observation could weaken.

This is the rationalist intuition Russell will not surrender. There are truths that hold not because the world happens to be one way rather than another, but because they could not have been otherwise. Empiricism alone cannot account for them.

## VIII. How A Priori Knowledge Is Possible

How can we know something independently of experience? Russell's answer is that a priori knowledge does not concern particular existing things. It concerns relations between universals. Two and two make four is a relation between the universals two and four, not a generalization about pairs of apples. It is true whether or not any pairs exist.

This shifts the question from psychology to ontology. The mind does not impose these truths on a reality that might otherwise be different. The truths hold in a domain that contains no particulars at all. The mind discovers them by directing itself to that domain.

Here Russell stands close to Plato without claiming to. The realm of universals is not in space, not in time, not in any mind. It is. The mind can attend to it. The truths found there are necessary because they describe what cannot be otherwise, anywhere.

## IX. The World of Universals

Russell now defends the reality of universals. Whiteness, justice, similarity, betweenness — these are not particulars. No one trips over justice. No instrument measures similarity itself, only similar things. Yet without universals, no meaningful statement can be made.

Consider the relation larger than. It is not located anywhere. It is not made of anything. It does not change. But it holds between particular things, and we recognize it again and again across new instances. The same relation, the same universal, is at work each time.

The medieval debate between realism and nominalism returns here. Are universals real, or only names we apply to grouped particulars? Russell argues for realism. Without genuine universals, the structure of language and the possibility of thought collapse. Even the nominalist who says only particulars exist must use the universal exists to say it.

This is metaphysical. Russell, often called an anti-metaphysician, here commits to a Platonic position. There is a realm of being beyond particulars, and the mind can reach it.

## X. On Our Knowledge of Universals

How do we come to know universals? Russell answers that universals are themselves objects of acquaintance. When I see two white things and recognize the whiteness in each, I am acquainted not only with the particulars but with the universal they share. Abstraction is not invention. It is attention.

This allows him to extend the principle from chapter five. We are acquainted with sense-data, with memories, with introspection, and now also with universals. Knowledge of any kind ultimately traces back to acquaintance with something, and that something may be particular or universal.

The implications run deep. The mind has access to two worlds. One is the world of changing particulars, given through sense. The other is the world of unchanging universals, given through what Russell calls conceiving. The philosopher works in both.

## XI. On Intuitive Knowledge

Not all knowledge is derived. Some truths are seen directly. That a thing cannot both be and not be in the same respect at the same time is not deduced from anything else. It is intuited as self-evident. Without such intuitive starting points, no chain of reasoning could begin. Every proof would require a prior proof, and nothing would ever be known.

Russell admits this is uncomfortable. Intuition seems unreliable. People have intuited absurd things. He distinguishes degrees of self-evidence. Some truths are luminous and indubitable. Others appear evident but on examination turn out to be false. Intuition is the ground of knowledge but also fallible.

The honest position is this: reasoning must end somewhere, and where it ends is in direct seeing. This is not a defect of reason. It is the structure of reason. Even the most rigorous proof, traced back, lands in an axiom that is not itself proved but seen.

## XII. Truth and Falsehood

What is truth? Russell defends the correspondence theory. A belief is true when it corresponds to a fact in the world. A belief is false when it fails to correspond. Truth is not coherence among beliefs, not utility, not consensus. It is a relation between mind and world.

But what is a fact? Russell describes facts as the obtaining of relations among objects. The fact that the cat is on the mat consists of a real cat standing in the relation of being on with respect to a real mat. The belief that the cat is on the mat is true if and only if this fact obtains.

This is a robustly realist position. Truth does not depend on whether anyone believes. The fact obtains or does not, regardless of recognition. Error is real because the world is real. Without an independent world, neither truth nor error has anything to be about.

## XIII. Knowledge, Error, and Probable Opinion

Most of what we think we know is not knowledge in the strict sense. It is probable opinion. Knowledge requires true belief grounded in something that connects belief to fact. Mere true belief is not enough. A guess that happens to match reality is not knowledge.

Russell distinguishes three states. There is knowledge, true belief properly grounded. There is error, false belief mistaken for true. And there is probable opinion, belief held with justification but short of certainty. Most of science, most of history, most of daily life, falls into the third category.

To recognize this is not skepticism. It is intellectual honesty. The mind that confuses probable opinion with knowledge is dogmatic. The mind that confuses both with error is paralyzed. The philosophical mind holds each in its proper place.

## XIV. The Limits of Philosophical Knowledge

Philosophy cannot answer all the questions it can ask. This is Russell's mature recognition. Many of the great metaphysical systems claim too much. They derive grand conclusions from premises that cannot bear the weight. Hegel claimed to deduce the structure of all reality from the concept of being. Russell finds the deduction unconvincing and the conclusions unwarranted.

What remains is more modest but more secure. Philosophy can clarify concepts. It can examine the foundations of knowledge. It can reveal the structure of language and thought. It can mark the boundary between what can be known and what cannot.

This is not a defeat. It is a discipline. Russell's philosophy is the long apprenticeship in not claiming more than one is entitled to claim. The recognition of limits is itself a form of knowledge, perhaps the most important form.

## XV. The Value of Philosophy

The book ends with a defense. Why study philosophy at all, if it cannot deliver certainty about ultimate things? Russell answers that the value of philosophy lies not in definite answers but in the questions themselves.

Philosophy enlarges the mind. It frees thought from the tyranny of custom and the narrowness of personal interest. It reveals that the familiar world contains depths and strangenesses that ordinary life conceals. It restores wonder to a mind that habit has dulled.

The closing sentences are among the most moving in twentieth-century philosophy. Through the freedom and impartiality of philosophical contemplation, Russell writes, the mind becomes capable of that union with the universe which constitutes its highest good. The self is enlarged by the world it contemplates. Philosophy does not give us mastery. It gives us communion.

## What Russell Leaves Open

Russell ends in modesty, but the modesty is itself a position, and a position can be questioned.

He grants the reality of universals but refuses the Platonic ascent. The realm of universals exists, but the contemplation of it does not transform the contemplator. For Plato, to know the Good was to be drawn toward it. For Russell, to know the universal is to record its relations. Knowledge stays cool.

He grants intuitive knowledge but never asks what makes intuition possible. The mind that sees that contradictions cannot obtain — what is the nature of that mind? Russell does not ask. The phenomenologists who came after, Husserl and Heidegger, made this their starting question.

He grants the limits of philosophy but draws those limits with a confidence that the limits themselves cannot justify. To say that ultimate metaphysics is impossible is itself an ultimate metaphysical claim. Wittgenstein, who learned from Russell, would later trouble this with his ladder that must be thrown away after one has climbed it.

And he treats the self that does the knowing as relatively unproblematic, acquainted with its own sense-data and its own thoughts. Hume had already shown this self to be elusive. The Buddhist tradition had shown it to be empty. Nagarjuna would ask Russell whether the knower he relies on so calmly is itself one more construction, dependent and without intrinsic existence.

These are not refutations. They are the next questions. Russell wrote a book that, by its honesty about what it could not do, opened the ground for everything that came after. This is the proper work of philosophy. Not to close the question. To pass it forward, sharpened.
