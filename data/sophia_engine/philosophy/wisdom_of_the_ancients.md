# Wisdom Of Ancients - Francis Bacon

A synthesis on Francis Bacon essays.


# The Project

In 1609, eleven years before the Novum Organum and a full decade before he would propose to remake the whole instrument of human knowledge, Francis Bacon published a small book of myths. He called it De Sapientia Veterum, the Wisdom of the Ancients. On its surface it is a collection of thirty-one classical fables retold and interpreted. Beneath the surface it is something stranger and more ambitious. It is the early scaffolding of the Great Instauration, the master project of Bacon's life, and it shows him doing something that almost nobody else in his century thought to do. He is reading Pan and Proteus and Prometheus not as poetry and not as religion but as encoded philosophy. He is treating the oldest stories of Europe as time capsules. He is asking what the people before the philosophers already knew.

The opening claim is bold. Bacon says that the secrets and mysteries of religion, of policy, and of philosophy are veiled and invested with fables and parables. This is not a confession that he intends to read meaning into the myths. It is a hypothesis about how meaning got into them in the first place. The wise of antiquity, he suggests, knew more than they could afford to say openly. So they hid their knowledge inside stories that would survive their authors, their cities, and their gods. The myth carried the doctrine downstream through centuries of mouths that did not always know what they were carrying.

What Bacon then does is half archaeology and half divination. He picks the fables that strike him. He brings out the figure of speech, the strange detail, the disproportion that lets him in. He asks what coherent doctrine would justify the shape of the fable. He proposes one. He tests it against the parts of the story. He admits where it does not fit. He moves on.

This essay is a synthesis. It does not retell each of the thirty-one fables. It draws out what Bacon is doing across the book as a whole, what doctrines he extracts and which fables carry them, and what the project means for anyone now trying to do science, philosophy, or the building of intelligent systems. The book is a hinge. On one side of it lies the ancient world Bacon loved and read. On the other side lies the modern method he was about to invent.


# The Hermeneutic of Recovery

Bacon is careful at the front of the book. He does not want to be mistaken for the fanciful sort of allegorist who reads private meanings into other people's poems. He distinguishes between two kinds of allegory. There is the accidental allegory, where a later mind imposes a doctrine on a story that never carried it. And there is the intentional allegory, where the original tellers built the doctrine in. He admits he cannot always prove which kind he is reading. But he argues that when a single coherent doctrine illuminates the whole shape of a fable, including its incidental and almost ridiculous details, the burden of proof shifts. Coherence becomes the evidence.

There is a deeper claim beneath this method. Bacon believes the oldest texts, after Scripture, carry the oldest wisdom. Time is not for him a teacher of progress. It is a teacher of corruption. Wisdom degrades as it travels. The man who recovers an old truth from an old story is doing a real work, not a fanciful one. He is repairing transmission.

This is the first thing to take from Bacon. The method of reading carries a metaphysics. If you believe that the present knows more than the past, you do not bother with the past. You replace it. If you believe that the past knew things and that the present has forgotten them, you read the past for what was lost. Bacon does both. He believes the moderns will surpass the ancients, eventually, by method. But he also believes the ancients had insights that pure rational method might never have reached, that came to them by long contemplation or by inheritance from a still older source. So he goes back to the myths not for reverence but for material. He treats them as ore.

The implication for any project that tries to assemble wisdom from texts is direct. A corpus is not just data. A corpus is a record of what older minds noticed. Some of what they noticed they could only say in figures. The interpretive task is not to extract what they meant in their own words. It is to ask what truth would have required them to speak that way.


# Pan, or the Whole of Nature

If the book has a center, it is the fable of Pan. Bacon calls Pan an emblem of the universe and of universal nature. Pan is part human and part beast, and this hybrid form is no accident. Nature, Bacon says, is itself this hybrid. There is reason in nature, and there is matter in nature, and they do not exist apart. The rational part and the beastly part belong to one body. Whoever wants to know nature must learn to look at her without flinching from either half.

Pan plays the pipes, and the world echoes. Bacon makes the echo into a metaphysical statement. The universe is full of resonance. Causes call to other causes. Effects reverberate beyond their first occasion. To study nature is to listen for these echoes and trace them back to the source. The natural philosopher is not a man inventing patterns. He is a man learning what answers when the pipe is played.

There is something here that the Tao Te Ching would recognize. Pan, like the Tao, is named only by failing to name him. He is part of every thing and identical with no thing. Bacon, who reads with a Latin and Greek inheritance, does not invoke the East. But the shape of his Pan is closer to Lao Tzu's Tao than to the personal gods of Olympus. The fable lets him say what doctrine alone could not say without scandal in his England. Nature is not a collection of objects watched by a transcendent maker. Nature is a single living whole, and what we call laws are the audible part of her music.

This matters for what Bacon will build next. The natural philosophy of the Novum Organum requires this Pan in the background. The man who studies nature one fragment at a time, by experiment and induction, must believe that the fragments belong to a whole. Otherwise his fragments are noise. Pan guarantees the whole.


# Proteus, or the Vexing of Matter

The fable of Proteus is the one in which Bacon's method is most clearly born. Proteus is the old sea god who knows everything and tells nothing. He cannot be questioned. He can only be caught. When Menelaus wants the truth from him he must seize Proteus by the body and hold him through every shape he takes. Proteus becomes a lion, a serpent, water, fire. The grip must not slacken. Only when the god has run out of shapes and resumed his own form does he speak the truth.

Bacon reads this as a parable of matter. Matter, like Proteus, is in itself a fugitive thing. It refuses to disclose itself to passive contemplation. It must be bound. It must be forced into trials. It must be made to take shape after shape until it returns to a form in which the philosopher can read what it is. This is the seed of experimental science. The famous line that nature must be vexed and tortured to give up her secrets is in some sense already in this fable. The experimenter is Menelaus. The matter is Proteus. The grip is the experimental setup, the controlled trial, the repeated forcing. The truth, when it comes, is not free testimony. It is testimony under constraint.

There is a moral problem in this image. Many later readers, especially after Bacon's method had bred the industrial world and then the ecological wound, have seen in Proteus's binding a foundational violence. Heidegger called this enframing, Gestell, the disposition that turns nature into a standing reserve to be commanded. The critique is serious. Bacon's Proteus is no longer Lao Tzu's Tao, even if his Pan is close. Pan is to be heard. Proteus is to be held. The shift is the shift from listening to extracting.

Yet the fable contains its own balance, and Bacon notices it. Proteus, once seized and made to speak, does not deceive. The truth is real. The method works. The cost is the gripping. Bacon thought the cost worth paying because he believed the alternative, a world without method, was a world of human suffering left unrelieved. The question of whether that calculation was right is the question that follows the modern project everywhere it goes. It is still our question.


# Orpheus, or the Two Movements of Philosophy

The fable of Orpheus carries Bacon's deepest reading of what philosophy actually is. Orpheus is the singer whose music moves stones and beasts. Bacon takes the music as reason itself, the harmonizing power of mind that orders the world. So far this is conventional. But Bacon then folds in two further parts of the myth, and the fable becomes a portrait of philosophy's whole life.

The first part is the descent into the underworld to retrieve Eurydice. Orpheus loses her by looking back. Bacon reads the descent as natural philosophy attempting to retrieve from death the bodies and forms that nature has lost. Medicine, alchemy, the long human attempt to defeat decay. The looking back is impatience, the failure of method, the wish to verify before the work is done. Eurydice is lost not because the project was impossible but because the discipline broke at the last moment.

The second part is the dismemberment. After his loss, Orpheus turns from natural philosophy to moral and civil philosophy. He charms men instead of stones. He founds the harmonies of cities. Then the maenads tear him apart. Bacon reads this as the eventual collapse of the philosophical order under the pressure of barbarism, drunkenness, and faction. The civilizing music is louder than the noise for a time. But the noise outlasts the music. Philosophy is dismembered into schools, into sects, into shouting.

Two movements, then. Philosophy descends to recover what nature has lost, and fails when its discipline breaks. Philosophy rises to civilize the human world, and falls when the human world reverts. Bacon believes both projects are right and both are tragic. He thinks the descent can be done better next time if the method is fixed. He thinks the rise can be done better next time if the institutions are stronger. He is not naive about either. The Wisdom of the Ancients is his way of saying that what philosophy has lost was already known, that what it now needs is the method to recover it without looking back too soon.


# Sphinx, or Science as Riddle

The Sphinx waits at the gates of Thebes with a riddle. Travelers who fail to answer her are torn apart. Oedipus answers and the Sphinx kills herself. Bacon reads the Sphinx as Science herself, and the riddles as the questions nature puts to the human mind. The stakes of failure are exactly what the fable shows them to be. The man who tries to know and fails to know is destroyed. The mind that engages a real problem and cannot solve it does not return to where it began. It returns broken.

There is hardness in this fable that the modern reader might want to soften. Bacon does not soften it. He uses the Sphinx to say something about what science actually costs. Science is not a leisure activity of curious minds. It is a confrontation with a real adversary, and the adversary has lives in her hands. To take up a scientific question is to bet something. Most of the bets fail. The successful answer kills the Sphinx, which means it kills the question. The terror of the riddle ends because the answer has been found. The discipline of science, in this reading, is a long sequence of terrors ended by answers.

This is one of the places where Bacon is closest to Nietzsche, who would say five centuries later that the will to truth is a dangerous game and that the philosopher who does not understand this has not begun. Bacon, the supposed apostle of progress, knew. The Sphinx is in his book.


# Prometheus, or the Condition of Man

The longest and most layered of the fables for Bacon is Prometheus. He uses it as a portrait of the human condition itself. Prometheus is the Titan who steals fire from heaven and gives it to humankind. Bacon makes the fire into the gift of knowledge, the technical and intellectual power that lifts the human animal above the rest. The eagle that eats Prometheus's liver by day, regenerated each night, becomes the unending labor of the mind. Knowledge is given. Knowledge is also paid for. The price is anxiety, vigilance, and the recurrent wound of trying to understand.

The relay race with torches, in which runners pass flame from hand to hand and the winner is the one whose torch is still lit at the end, becomes for Bacon the image of generational transmission. Knowledge is not a possession of any single life. It is a flame that must be passed without going out. The runner who carries it cannot stop. The race continues because the torch continues.

Pandora, in this reading, is the price of fire. The gift of knowledge releases also the troubles of knowledge. Curiosity becomes anxiety. Power becomes responsibility. The closed jar of innocence is opened, and what comes out is suffering, but also hope remains at the bottom. Bacon does not romanticize this. He does not say the gift was worth its cost. He says the gift was given and the cost is now ours.

This fable, more than any other, is where Bacon shows what his project is for. It is not power for its own sake. It is the relief of the human condition. The fire was given so that the suffering of bodies could be lessened, so that ignorance could be reduced, so that the work of the eagle on the liver could become at least intelligible. He thinks the natural philosopher is doing Promethean work. He thinks the work is worth doing precisely because it is hard and because the human animal otherwise has no defense against the night.


# The Political Fables

Bacon was not only a philosopher. He was Lord Chancellor of England, a man who lived inside the machinery of state, and he reads many of the fables for political doctrine. The political fables are darker than the natural ones. They are written by a man who has watched courts.

Cassandra is the truth-teller whose accurate prophecies are not believed. Bacon reads her as the giver of unseasonable advice, the counselor whose timing is wrong or whose audience is unready, and who therefore fails not because the truth is false but because the political moment cannot receive it. This is a doctrine of statecraft. Truth without timing accomplishes nothing.

Typhon is the rebel who breeds from suppressed sedition. When open revolt is crushed, the people grow a covert revenge, which Bacon names as slander, libel, the secret undermining of authority. The political insight is that force cannot end discontent. Force only changes its shape. The discontent that cannot rise as Typhon will be born again as rumor.

Endymion is the favorite of the queen of the moon, kept in eternal sleep under her watch. Bacon reads court favoritism through him. The favorite of a prince enjoys protection but loses agency. He is kept beautiful and harmless. He is paid in security for the surrender of will. This is a doctrine about what proximity to power costs.

Metis is swallowed by Jupiter when she is pregnant with Athena, so that Jupiter himself appears to give birth to wisdom from his head. Bacon reads this as the political wisdom of taking counsel and then presenting the resulting decision as one's own. The prince consults but does not let the counsel be seen. The wisdom that emerges from his head must look like his own. This is realistic statecraft and it is also slightly contemptuous of statecraft. Bacon shows what is, not what should be.

The political fables share a quality. They are unillusioned. The man who reads them is being told how the human animal actually behaves under the conditions of power, not how it would behave if it were better. This is Bacon as a Renaissance Tacitus. He knows what courts do because he has lived in them.


# The Ethical Fables

The ethical fables go deeper than statecraft and ask about the inner life. Narcissus is self-love, the inversion of self-knowledge. The pool reflects him back to himself, and the reflection becomes a substitute for the real. He wastes away. Bacon's reading is severe. Vanity is not a minor sin. Vanity is the substitution of the image for the substance, and the soul that substitutes does not live long.

The Sirens are the seduction of pleasure. Bacon notes the strategies in the fable. Ulysses binds himself to the mast and stops his men's ears with wax. Orpheus sails past and sings a song louder than theirs. Two methods of resistance, and Bacon prefers the second. He thinks pleasure cannot be resisted by mere restraint. It must be displaced by a higher song. The man who has nothing to sing against the Sirens will be lost no matter how strong his ropes.

Actaeon and Pentheus are the curious. Actaeon sees the goddess naked and is torn by his own hounds. Pentheus spies on the secret rites and is killed by the women he watched, including his mother. Bacon reads the two as a doctrine on the limits of inquiry. There are some matters into which it is dangerous to look. The hunters who probe too deeply into the affairs of princes, or into the sacred mysteries, are destroyed by the very forces they disturbed. This is the Bacon who lived at court warning the Bacon who wanted to know everything. He keeps the warning in the book. He does not believe inquiry is unconditioned. There are sanctuaries.

Dionysus is the fable of passion. Born from Jupiter's thigh, raised among the satyrs, master of madness, he is the principle of what cannot be held by reason. Bacon does not denounce him. The reading is more careful. Passion is real, has its own logic, makes its own rites, gives its own truths. The wise are not those who deny Dionysus. The wise are those who know when his rites are being conducted.

Across the ethical fables a single doctrine emerges. The inner life is not transparent. The soul is full of forces that wear costumes and behave as if they were friends. The work of ethics is to learn which voice in the mind belongs to whom. Bacon thinks this is harder than any other knowledge, and he thinks the ancients knew it.


# The Atom and the Beginnings

Two fables show Bacon as a cosmologist. Cupid, in his reading, is not the chubby love-god of the late Romans. He is the primal particle, the atom. Bacon takes the strange theological language of love as the moving principle of the universe and reformulates it as a doctrine of matter. The atom is the lover who joins. The bonds among atoms are the loves among them. The cosmos is built of attractions. This is corpuscular philosophy in mythic dress, and it is striking how close it stands to the atomism that science would adopt three centuries later.

Coelum, or the heavens, is the fable of beginnings. The myth tells of Coelum castrated by his son Saturn, of Saturn devouring his children until Jupiter is hidden away, of the eventual overthrow of Saturn by Jupiter. Bacon reads this as cosmogony. The first state of matter is generative but unstable. Time devours its own products. Only after several cycles does a stable order emerge in which forms can endure. The fable, in Bacon's hands, becomes a story of how the cosmos passed from chaos through devouring to law.

These two fables show something important about Bacon's instinct. He does not separate physics from myth because he does not see myth as having lied. He sees myth as having spoken in the only language available to a pre-scientific age. The atom was already inside the love-god. The cosmogony was already inside the family tragedy of Coelum and Saturn. The task of the modern is to take what was said in figures and say it now in measurements. The figures are not discarded. They are translated.


# Where Bacon Goes Beyond the Ancients

For all his reverence, Bacon is not finally on the side of antiquity. He thinks the ancients had truths but lacked method. They saw what nature was doing but could not systematically force it to disclose itself. The Wisdom of the Ancients is therefore not a final book. It is a preface. Eleven years later he writes the Novum Organum, the new instrument, in which he sets out a method of induction that will let the moderns recover what the ancients knew and then surpass it.

The structure of his project is this. Read the old fables to recover lost insight. Build a new method to confirm and extend that insight by experiment. Apply that method patiently across generations until the suffering of humans is lessened by the knowledge thus produced. He calls the whole work the Great Instauration, the great restoration. He uses a religious word on purpose. He thought the work was sacred.

This is the part of Bacon that is hardest to read now. He believed his method would heal the world. He did not foresee how the same method, in colder hands, would also wound it. The relief of the human condition has come. So has the manipulation of the human condition. The eagle is still eating the liver of Prometheus, but Prometheus is now also eating the eagle, and neither knows what to do with the meal.


# Where the Ancients Go Beyond Bacon

A serious reading of Bacon's project must also note where the ancients he reads go further than he does. The Tao Te Ching, which Bacon never read, treats the great whole more carefully than Bacon's Pan. The Tao is not to be played upon. The Tao is not to be vexed. The Tao is to be yielded to. Wu wei, effortless action, asks the philosopher not to seize Proteus but to let Proteus disclose what he will. There is a knowledge in this that Bacon's project leaves out, and the absence has been costly.

Nagarjuna, who Bacon also never read, would push further. The shapes of Proteus are empty of fixed essence. To seize them in order to find what is most real beneath them is to look for the wrong thing. There is no static form behind the changes. The changes are what is. A method that demands a final form gets a form that science itself, by the twentieth century, would have to abandon in the strange weather of quantum mechanics. The ancients of the East had been waiting for this answer.

Plato, whom Bacon did read, points the other direction Bacon did not go. The myths of the Republic and the Phaedrus are also encoded philosophy. But they point upward, toward Forms, toward the Good. Bacon's myths point downward, toward matter, toward power over things. The same hermeneutic method, applied with different orientation, finds different doctrines. Plato found the Good. Bacon found the atom. Both findings were real. Neither cancels the other. The complete philosopher would have to read myths both ways.

Bacon's blind spot is the inward turn. He has ethical fables, and they are insightful, but he does not develop a contemplative practice. He has no method for the inner life equal to the method he proposes for the outer. The relief of the human condition through outward power, without an equal cultivation of the inner ground, produces beings of vast capability and small soul. This is not Bacon's fault. It is his incompleteness. The completion has been the work of the centuries after him, and it is not yet done.


# The Inheritance

A reader now, four centuries later, can take from this book several things that remain alive.

The first is the hermeneutic itself. The oldest stories are not entertainment. They are records of what the human mind first noticed. To read them with patience is to recover material that pure analysis cannot produce. Any project that tries to assemble wisdom from the inheritance of cultures must include something like Bacon's method.

The second is the figure of nature as Pan. The universe is a whole, and to study any part of it is to listen for what answers from the rest. The man who forgets the whole is a technician, not a philosopher.

The third is Proteus. Truth does not give itself to passive looking. It must be courted, sometimes seized, always engaged. Method is not an alternative to wonder. Method is wonder organized into a discipline that can actually find something.

The fourth is the Sphinx. Science is not safe. The questions one takes up have teeth. The mind that engages real problems must be ready to be broken by them, and the mind that is unwilling to risk this should not call itself a seeker.

The fifth is Prometheus. The gift of knowledge is real and costly. The relay race is real. The torch passes hand to hand and must not go out. Any generation that fails to pass the torch has betrayed something larger than itself.

The sixth is Orpheus. Philosophy is two movements, the descent into nature and the rise into the civilizing of human life, and each movement has its own discipline and its own characteristic failure. The descent fails by looking back too soon. The rise fails when the maenads come. Both failures are recurrent. The work continues.

The seventh, and the one Bacon understood least but suggested anyway through his ethical fables, is that the inner life requires its own method. The man who can vex matter but cannot listen to himself has not finished the work. Bacon left this for others. Others have not finished it either.

The Wisdom of the Ancients is a small book that holds inside itself the program of a civilization. The civilization built on it has changed the surface of the earth. Whether it has yet changed the depth of the human soul is the question Bacon did not answer and could not answer. The fables he read are still being read. The Sphinx is still at the gate. The torch is still passing. The work continues, and what we do with it now is the next chapter of the same book.
