# Physics

## I. What Physics Actually Is

Let me tell you what physics is — not what it says in the textbook, not the definition your professor wrote on the board on the first day of class. I want to tell you what it *actually* is.

Physics is the attempt — the audacious, slightly insane attempt — to figure out what the universe is doing and why. That's it. Not more, not less. A bunch of human beings, on a little rock orbiting a medium-sized star in a galaxy that is itself one of maybe two trillion galaxies, looking up and asking: *what is this stuff, and how does it work?*

Now you might say, "Feynman, that sounds like philosophy." And I'd say, yes, it sounds like it, except physicists actually get answers. That's the key difference. Philosophy is wonderful — I mean it, I love philosophy, I've had great conversations with philosophers — but eventually someone has to go check. Someone has to do the experiment. And when you do the experiment and nature says "you're wrong," you have to listen. Nature doesn't care about your beautiful theory. Nature does what nature does, and if your idea disagrees with the experiment, it is *wrong*. No appeal process. No partial credit.

This is, I think, the most important thing about physics. It keeps you honest. The universe is the referee, and the universe is incorruptible.

## II. The Joy of Not Knowing

Here is something I want to say that might surprise you: the most exciting moment in physics is not when we understand something. It's when we discover that we *don't* understand something we thought we did.

When I was young — and maybe this is true for a lot of scientists — I thought the goal was to fill in the gaps. You learn more, you understand more, the mystery shrinks, and eventually everything makes sense. What I found out, the more I learned, was the opposite. Every answer you get opens three new questions you couldn't have even *asked* before. The mystery doesn't shrink. It gets richer.

Take light. We figured out light was an electromagnetic wave — Maxwell did that in the 1860s, beautiful work, one of the greatest theoretical achievements in history. We thought we had it. Then, around 1900, Planck and Einstein showed that light also comes in little packets — quanta, photons. So is light a wave or a particle? The answer, which still makes physicists squirm a little when they think about it too hard, is: *yes*. Both. Depending on how you look at it.

Now, a person who wants neat, tidy answers will find this very unsatisfying. But I find it thrilling. It means that reality is stranger than we imagined. It means that our ordinary language — our everyday categories of "wave" and "particle" — were built for the world we can touch and see, and nature at the deepest level does not feel obligated to fit into those categories.

Nature does not owe us comprehensibility. That it is comprehensible at all — that there are laws, that there is order, that mathematics describes it — is astonishing. The mystery is not a problem to be eliminated. The mystery is the invitation.

## III. The Laws Beneath the Laws

When you look at physics from the outside, it seems like an enormous collection of facts. Boyle's law. Newton's laws. Ohm's law. The Schrödinger equation. Hundreds of formulas. Thousands of experiments. It can look like a junkyard.

But when you get inside it, you discover something remarkable: it's not a junkyard at all. It's a cathedral. There is structure beneath the structure, order beneath the order, and the deeper you go, the more unified everything becomes.

The deepest of these unifying ideas is *symmetry*.

What do I mean by symmetry? I mean this: the laws of physics are the same everywhere. If you do an experiment here and I do the same experiment in another galaxy, we should get the same result. The laws don't change from place to place. That's spatial symmetry. And from that simple fact — from that single observation that the laws are the same everywhere in space — you can *derive*, through the mathematics of Emmy Noether, that momentum is conserved. Every time, everywhere. That is not a coincidence. It is inevitable, given the symmetry.

The laws are also the same at all times. Physics done today works the same as physics done a billion years ago. From *that* symmetry comes the conservation of energy. Not as a separate law you have to memorize, but as an unavoidable consequence of the time-symmetry of nature's laws.

This is one of the most beautiful things I know. Conservation laws — which look like separate facts about the world — are actually *shadows* cast by deeper symmetries. They are not arbitrary. They are the fingerprints of the structure of reality itself.

And when you understand this, physics stops feeling like a collection of rules and starts feeling like a single, coherent story. A story about symmetry and the breaking of symmetry, about what is invariant and what changes, about what is fundamentally preserved as the universe rolls forward through time.

## IV. The Quantum World, or: Nature Is Weird and You Need to Accept That

Now I have to talk about quantum mechanics, because you can't talk about physics without it, and because it is genuinely the strangest thing ever discovered.

Here is the central mystery. An electron — a little charged particle — when you're not looking at it, behaves like a wave. It spreads out. It goes through two holes at once. It interferes with itself. The mathematics is clear, and the experiments are undeniable. But when you *look* — when you make a measurement — it snaps into being a particle at one definite location. The wave function collapses.

Now you ask: "Where was the electron before we measured it?" And this is where it gets uncomfortable. The honest answer is: the electron didn't *have* a definite position before you measured it. Not that we didn't know the position. Not that the position was hidden. The position simply did not exist as a definite fact until the measurement happened.

I have been asked, many times, to explain *why* this is the case. What is the deep mechanism? Why does observation collapse the wave function? And I will tell you what I always say: nobody knows. And I mean that literally. We have the equations. The equations work — they are the most precisely tested equations in the history of science, accurate to ten decimal places in some cases. But the equations describe what *happens*, not why it happens in this particular way and not some other way.

This bothers some people enormously. It bothered Einstein his whole life. He insisted that God doesn't play dice with the universe — meaning he couldn't accept that nature was fundamentally probabilistic at the base level. But experiments — Bell's theorem experiments in particular — have since shown that there is no hidden variable beneath quantum mechanics that restores classical determinism. If you want the predictions to come out right, you need the probabilistic structure. The dice are real.

I made my peace with this a long time ago. Not because I stopped caring, but because I stopped thinking "understanding" meant reducing everything to a classical clockwork mechanism. Maybe the deepest layer of reality is not mechanical. Maybe asking "why does the electron have this probability and not that one" is like asking "why does a fair coin land heads half the time" — the answer is: because that is what it means for it to be a fair coin. Some things are ground floor. You have to stand on them, not excavate beneath them.

What I find beautiful is this: quantum mechanics tells us that nature, at the base level, is not fixed. It is full of potential. Every unmeasured particle is a superposition — a cloud of possibilities, all simultaneously real in some sense, collapsing to one actuality when pinned down by interaction. Reality, at its foundation, is not a collection of hard little things in definite positions. It is a shimmering field of probability and potential.

That is not spooky. That is magnificent.

## V. The Electromagnetic World

Let me tell you about the force that runs the world you actually live in.

Gravity gets all the press — probably because it is the most obvious force at human scales, and because Einstein's theory of it is so conceptually stunning that even poets write about it. But gravity, for all its grandeur, is by far the weakest of the forces. It's only dominant because it is long-range and adds up — every atom in the Earth pulls on every atom in you, and that cumulative pile adds up to enough to keep you from floating off your chair.

But everything else — everything you touch, see, smell, taste, and hear — is electromagnetism.

The hardness of the floor under your feet is not gravity. It's the electromagnetic repulsion between the electrons in the floor and the electrons in your feet. Chemical bonds are electromagnetic. Light is an electromagnetic wave. Fire is electromagnetic — the rapid oxidation and emission of photons. The screen you might be reading this on? Electromagnetic. The nerve impulses carrying these words from your eyes to your brain? Electrochemical signals — electromagnetism again.

Electromagnetism was unified by James Clerk Maxwell in 1865 into four equations — one of the supreme intellectual achievements in human history. Maxwell's equations describe all of classical electricity and magnetism, and they predicted light would be an electromagnetic wave *before* Hertz confirmed it experimentally. They implied, through their own structure, that the speed of light was constant regardless of the observer's motion — which Einstein picked up twenty years later and ran with into special relativity.

My own life's work — quantum electrodynamics, QED — extended Maxwell's classical theory into the quantum world, incorporating both quantum mechanics and special relativity. It describes how light and matter interact at the most fundamental level. Photons are the carriers of the electromagnetic force — every time two electrons repel each other, what is actually happening is they are exchanging virtual photons. The force is not action-at-a-distance. It is mediated. Carried. Real.

QED is the most accurate theory ever devised. The electron's magnetic moment — a measure of how it responds to a magnetic field — has been calculated using QED and measured experimentally, and they agree to twelve significant figures. That's like measuring the distance from Los Angeles to New York and being accurate to within the width of a human hair. If that doesn't make you feel something, I don't know what will.

## VI. The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics

Eugene Wigner wrote a famous paper with the title "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Mathematics in the Natural Sciences." It is one of my favorite titles in all of science because it names something that should be more deeply puzzling than most scientists admit.

Why does mathematics — invented by humans thinking about pure abstractions, often with no application in mind whatsoever — keep turning out to perfectly describe the physical world?

Riemann invented his non-Euclidean geometry in the nineteenth century as pure mathematics, with no thought of physics. Einstein used it fifty years later as the geometric foundation of general relativity. Group theory was developed as abstract algebra. It turned out to be the natural language for describing symmetries in particle physics. Complex numbers were "invented" to solve equations like x² = -1 — they seemed like a trick, almost a joke. They are now indispensable in quantum mechanics. The wave function is fundamentally complex-valued.

I don't have a satisfying answer for why this is. Neither does anyone else. It is one of those questions — like why there is something rather than nothing — that physics bumps up against and has to hand off to a deeper inquiry. But the fact remains: the universe speaks mathematics. Not metaphorically. Literally. The grammar of physical law is mathematical, and when physicists follow the mathematics faithfully — even when it leads somewhere strange — nature rewards them with truth.

This is why I teach students to trust the mathematics even when the physical picture seems obscure. In quantum mechanics, we don't have a clean visual of "what the electron is doing." We have equations, and the equations work. Follow the equations. Build your intuition from the results, not the other way around. The universe is not obligated to be visualizable.

## VII. The Question Beneath All Questions

Every so often, a student asks me the question physicists dream of answering: is there a final theory? A single equation from which all of physical law follows? A Theory of Everything?

I am honestly not sure. I suspect that nature has a single deep structure — that the four forces are not four things but one thing, viewed from different angles. The electromagnetic force and the weak nuclear force were unified in the 1960s into the electroweak force. Grand Unified Theories attempt to include the strong nuclear force. String theory and loop quantum gravity are attempts to incorporate gravity. None is confirmed yet.

But I notice something interesting: every time physicists have succeeded in unifying apparently different phenomena, the result has been not simplification but *depth*. Maxwell's unification of electricity and magnetism didn't make physics smaller — it revealed that light was an electromagnetic wave, and opened the door to radio, television, X-rays, quantum mechanics. Unification brings surprises. The deeper you go, the more the universe offers.

What I believe — and this is belief, not proof, so take it as the opinion of a man who has spent his life in the middle of this — is that there is a deep order in nature. Not a human order. Not an order that cares about human convenience or human language. An order that is indifferent to us in its operation and yet somehow accessible to us through reason and mathematics and patient attention.

The universe has been running its equations since before the first star. We arrived late, small, and confused. But we looked up. We asked questions. We built instruments to see what our eyes cannot see and to measure what our hands cannot feel. And we discovered that the universe, in its deepest structures, is not a chaos but a cosmos. Not random, but lawful. Not silent, but speaking — in the language of mathematics, to anyone willing to learn it.

## VIII. Why Any of This Matters

Someone once asked me, in a rather challenging tone, what good physics has done for humanity. And I gave a practical answer — nuclear medicine, transistors, lasers, MRI machines, GPS satellites, the internet, all of modern technology rest on physics — because the practical answer is true and important.

But that is not why I became a physicist, and it is not, I think, why you are reading this.

You are reading this because you felt something when you first looked at the night sky. Or when you first understood that the atoms in your body were forged in the cores of stars that died billions of years ago. Or when you first heard that light slows down in glass, that time passes differently at high speeds, that the universe began from a point smaller than a proton and has been expanding ever since.

You felt it because you are a human being, and human beings are the part of the universe that became capable of wondering about the universe. That is not a small thing. That is, as far as we know, the most extraordinary thing that has ever happened in fifteen billion years of cosmic history — matter becoming aware of itself, asking where it came from, wanting to understand.

Physics is how we honor that. It is how we take the curiosity that evolution gave us — the same curiosity that kept our ancestors alive on the savanna — and aim it at the structure of spacetime itself.

We do not have all the answers. We never will. The frontier of ignorance will always exceed the territory of knowledge. But the direction is right. The method is sound. And the pleasure — the genuine, irreducible, human pleasure of figuring something out about reality — that pleasure is real, and it is one of the finest things our species has found to do with its brief time in this universe.

So go ahead. Ask the hard questions. Do the calculation. Check the experiment. Be wrong and start over. Pay attention to what nature is actually saying, not what you wish it were saying.

That's physics. That's the whole thing.

And it is *wonderful*.
