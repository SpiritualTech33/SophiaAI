# The First Philosophers Of Greece - Arthur Fairbanks

## The Question Asked Once

Something happened on the coast of Asia Minor in the sixth century before Christ that had never happened before. Men began to ask, in a way that left no room for stories, what the world is made of. Not who made it. Not whose anger explains the storm. Not which god lives in the spring. What is the thing itself, beneath the appearances, that lets there be a world at all.

This is the question philosophy is. Everything that follows in the Greek tradition, and through it everything in the larger Western inheritance, is a continuation of the work begun in Miletos, Ephesus, Elea, and Acragas. Fairbanks, in his patient nineteenth-century way, collected the fragments and testimonia that remain. The body of his book is a museum of broken speech. A sentence here, a paraphrase there, a hostile witness reporting what an enemy taught. What the book gives us is not a system but the moment a way of thinking was born.

What follows is a synthesis of that birth. The path runs from water to the boundless to air, then to number, then to a single god behind the gods, then to fire and flux, then to Being itself, then back through pluralities and Mind to the threshold of what Plato will inherit.

## The Milesian Move

Thales stands at the beginning because he asked the question in a form the question could answer. He said water. Everything is water. Earth floats on it. Living things grow from moisture. Heat itself, the strange opposite of wet, comes out of moist sources. The soul moves matter the way a magnet moves iron. There is a power inside things, and that power is somehow continuous with the wet substance of the world. All things, he said, are full of gods.

This is not yet science. It is also not mythology. It is the first time, in writing that has come down to us, that a man tried to give a single account of the many. To find one principle, one nature, one substance, that explains the rest. The Greek word for that principle is arche. First thing, ruling thing, source. Thales said the arche is water, and in saying so he made all subsequent philosophy possible. The content matters less than the form of the claim. To ask what everything is made of is already to believe that everything is one in some way, and that the one can be known.

Anaximander, his successor, saw what was wrong with the answer. If water is the arche, why does anything but water exist? Why is the cosmos not a simple ocean? Why is there fire, which is hot and dry, the opposite of water? The principle, Anaximander reasoned, cannot be one of the things it produces. It must be more abstract, more indeterminate, more capacious than any element we know. He called it the apeiron. The boundless, the unlimited, the indeterminate. From it everything emerges. To it everything returns. It has no beginning because it is the source of all beginnings. It has no end because nothing greater can swallow it.

The fragment Anaximander left us is one of the most extraordinary sentences in early thought. Things, he said, suffer punishment and give satisfaction to one another for their injustice according to the ordering of time. The cosmos is moral. The arising and passing away of beings is not random spillage but a measured exchange, each thing paying for its existence by ceasing to exist, returning to the boundless what it borrowed. This is metaphysics and ethics in the same sentence. To be at all is to commit a kind of trespass against the unity from which one came, and time itself is the court in which the debt is settled.

Anaximander also imagined the earth as a cylinder hanging unsupported at the center of all things, equidistant from everything, needing nothing beneath it because there is no reason it should fall in any one direction. This is one of the first arguments from sufficient reason in the history of thought. He imagined men originating as fish-like creatures, growing up in the wet, emerging onto dry land when they were strong enough. A remarkable foreshadowing of what biology would take twenty-five centuries to begin to say with care.

Anaximenes, the third Milesian, did something subtle. He returned to a determinate element, air, but kept the abstraction Anaximander had gained. Air, for Anaximenes, is not just one thing among others. It is the medium through which all transformation happens. When air rarefies, it becomes fire. When it condenses, it becomes wind, then cloud, then water, then earth, then stone. The whole material world is air at varying densities. Change is no longer mysterious. It is a single substance under varying pressure.

And he gave us the analogy that would shape Greek thought about the soul for centuries. As our soul, which is air, holds us together, so wind and air encompass the whole world. Breath is to the body what cosmic air is to the cosmos. The microcosm and the macrocosm are the same thing seen at different scales. Anaximenes is the first to make that move explicit. The man who breathes and the world that surrounds him are versions of one another.

The Milesians together accomplished something none of them alone could. They established that the world is intelligible, that beneath its variety there is one principle, that the principle can be named, that change is not chaos but transformation, and that the human being who asks is continuous with the cosmos he asks about. Everything that follows in Greek philosophy is a refinement of these claims, or a reaction against them, or both.

## The Number Behind the Thing

Pythagoras left the coast of Asia Minor for southern Italy, where he founded a community half-religious, half-mathematical. The Pythagoreans observed something the Milesians had not yet seen. When a string is plucked, the notes it produces correspond to whole-number ratios of its length. Halve the string, get the octave. Take two-thirds, get the fifth. Three-quarters, the fourth. The harmony of sounds, which moves the soul, is a mathematical fact. The world is not merely material. The world is mathematical. Number is the inner structure of everything that is.

From this observation grew a metaphysics. The Pythagoreans began to teach that all things are number, or that the elements of number are the elements of being. They distinguished the limit and the unlimited as the two ultimate principles. Form, definition, and intelligibility belong to the limit. Indeterminacy, infinity, and matter belong to the unlimited. To be a thing is to be a structured shaping of the unlimited by the limit. They constructed a table of opposites. Limit and unlimited, odd and even, one and many, right and left, male and female, rest and motion, straight and crooked, light and dark, good and evil, square and oblong. All reality is woven from these polarities.

They also taught that the soul does not perish. It moves from body to body, paying debts, accumulating knowledge, working toward purification. The body is a tomb in which the soul is confined for a time. Philosophy is one of the practices by which the soul is freed. Pythagoras is said to have recognized in the cry of a beaten puppy the voice of a friend who had died. A story that captures, perhaps in caricature, the seriousness with which they took the migration of souls.

What Pythagoras gave the tradition was the discovery that mathematics is not merely a tool for counting but a description of reality at its deepest level. The cosmos has a logos because the cosmos has a number. The harmony of the spheres, that famous Pythagorean phrase, is not poetry but ontology. The same ratios that govern the lyre govern the orbits of the heavens. To know the world is to know its proportions. Plato will take this insight and build the Timaeus on it. Galileo will take it again, two thousand years later, and say the book of nature is written in the language of mathematics. The Pythagoreans were the first to insist that to think the world is to count it.

## The God Above the Gods

Xenophanes of Colophon was an exile and a poet. He traveled the Greek world reciting verses, and what he saw and heard convinced him that the gods men worshipped were not gods at all but mirrors. The Ethiopians, he said, make their gods snub-nosed and black. The Thracians say theirs have blue eyes and red hair. If oxen and horses and lions had hands and could draw, they would draw the forms of gods like horses, and oxen like oxen. The gods of the Greeks steal, commit adultery, deceive one another, suffer the indignities Homer ascribes to them. Xenophanes refused this. Whatever the divine is, it is not this.

In place of the anthropomorphic crowd of Olympus he set a single principle. There is one god, greatest among gods and men, not like mortals in body or in thought. He neither comes nor goes. He abides. He sees as a whole, thinks as a whole, hears as a whole. Without effort, by the thought of his mind, he sets in motion the whole. This is not yet the God of the philosophers in the developed sense, but it is the first time in Greek thought that the divine has been stripped of human shape and human passion and placed at a distance that makes worship begin to look like contemplation.

Xenophanes also gave us one of the earliest fragments on the limits of human knowledge. The gods, he wrote, have not revealed all things to mortals from the beginning, but mortals by seeking find out better in time. This is one of the founding statements of an epistemology that takes inquiry seriously without claiming certainty. We do not know what the gods know. We can search. By searching we find better. Better is not best, and what we find now we may find more accurately later. There is room for science here, and room for humility, and the two are continuous.

He held also that all things that come into being and grow are earth and water. The cosmological picture is austere. What matters in Xenophanes is the purification of the divine and the licensing of inquiry. Both will travel into Plato, into the Stoics, and eventually into Aristotle's God who thinks himself thinking.

## The River and the Fire

Heraclitus wrote in Ephesus, on the same Ionian coast where the Milesians had begun their work, and the ancient world called him obscure for a reason. His sentences are riddles. He spoke in fragments because he believed reality itself was structured as paradox, and only paradox could speak it. Nature loves to hide. The wise must love what is hidden. So he wrote in flashes, and the flashes have lasted.

He chose fire as his image of the cosmos. The world, he said, neither any god nor any man made, but it always was and is and shall be, an ever-living fire, kindling in measures and going out in measures. Fire is the right image because fire is the principle that is always changing yet always recognizably itself. A flame is not the same atoms moment to moment, yet a flame is a flame. The cosmos is like that. It is identity through transformation. To exist is to burn.

The river follows from the fire. You cannot step twice in the same river, Heraclitus said, for other waters are ever flowing on. A river is the form of its flowing. If the water stopped, the river would not be preserved. It would be destroyed. The river is held together by what passes through it. So with everything. So with you. You are not a thing but a process. Your identity is constituted by your transformation. To stop changing is not to be preserved, but to be ended.

Beneath this constant change Heraclitus saw an order he called logos. The logos is the principle by which the burning is structured, the rule of the transformations, the reason inherent in things. Listening not to me but to the logos, he said, it is wise to acknowledge that all things are one. This logos is common, yet men live as though they had wisdom of their own. The fragments return to this complaint. The truth is open. The truth is structured. The truth is in plain hearing. And men miss it because they treat their private experience as the standard, when the logos is what is shared.

The doctrine of the unity of opposites runs through everything Heraclitus wrote. The way up and the way down are one and the same. The bow and the lyre work by tension between opposing forces. Their function is the harmony of their opposition. War, he said, is the father of all and the king of all. By this he did not mean that violence is good but that opposition is the structure within which anything stable comes to be. Without the tension between high and low there is no scale. Without the tension between heat and cold there is no temperature. Reality is the holding-together of what pulls apart.

The soul he treated as fire. The dry soul is wisest and best. Souls become wet when they are drunk, or sleepy, or sluggish, and they become foolish. To wake up is to dry out. To philosophize is to burn. The soul, in being kindled, participates in the cosmic fire, and to die well is to return to it well.

Heraclitus is the first true philosopher of becoming. He understood that the metaphysics of presence, the assumption that to be is to remain, could not survive contact with what actually exists. To be is to flow according to measure. To know that is to begin to be wise. Twenty-four centuries later the Tao Te Ching will be read alongside Heraclitus and the resonance will be unmistakable. The Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao. Nature loves to hide. These are the same insight in two languages, made in the same century on opposite sides of the world.

## What Cannot Be Said

Parmenides of Elea heard the question differently. If Heraclitus was the philosopher of flux, Parmenides was the philosopher who said flux is impossible, and his argument is so simple and so devastating that the entire rest of Greek philosophy can be read as an attempt to escape it.

His poem distinguishes two ways. There is the way of truth and the way of opinion. The way of truth proceeds by this principle. What is, is, and cannot not be. What is not, is not, and cannot be. Nothing can come from nothing, because nothing is nothing. Nothing can pass into nothing, because there is no such place as nothing for it to pass into. To say that a thing comes to be is to say that it was once not. But what is not is not, and so it could not have been, and so there is no place for the coming-to-be to begin. To say that a thing passes away is to say it becomes what is not. But what is not is not, and so there is nowhere for it to go.

The conclusion is staggering. Being is uncreated, indestructible, whole, motionless, indivisible, and one. There is no past in Being, because Being never was not. There is no future in Being, because Being never will not be. There is only the timeless presence of what is. Reason proves this. The senses, which show us change, are therefore deceived. The world we see is the way of opinion. The world we think, by argument, is the way of truth.

Parmenides also gave us, in one of his hardest fragments, the identity of thinking and being. For thinking and being are the same. You cannot think what is not, because what is not is nothing, and to think nothing is not to think. Every act of thought is an act about something. To think is to be in contact with what is. This is the first time in Greek philosophy that thought and reality are explicitly identified at their root. Two and a half millennia later Hegel will return to this identity and build a system on it.

Zeno of Elea was Parmenides' student and defender. He saw that the way of opinion, the world of plurality and motion, could be made to contradict itself by careful argument. Take motion. To get from here to there, you must first get halfway. To get halfway, you must first get a quarter of the way. To get a quarter of the way, an eighth. The process never bottoms out. Motion requires the traversal of infinitely many points in a finite time, which is absurd. Or take Achilles and the tortoise. Achilles runs faster, but the tortoise has a head start. By the time Achilles reaches the tortoise's starting point, the tortoise has moved ahead. By the time Achilles reaches that new point, the tortoise has moved ahead again. The series never closes. The swift cannot overtake the slow.

Or take the arrow. At any given instant of its flight, the arrow occupies a space exactly the size of itself. While it occupies that space, it is not moving. To move would be to be partly somewhere else. But the flight is composed of instants. If at every instant the arrow is not moving, when does it move? Motion, Zeno concludes, is an illusion.

Whatever one thinks of these arguments, and they have provoked work in mathematics and logic for two and a half thousand years, their force is real. Zeno demonstrated that the common-sense picture of the world contains contradictions when pressed. The Parmenidean claim that only Being is, and Being is one, is not a poetic flourish but a conclusion to which careful reasoning seems to drive us.

Melissus of Samos extended the argument geographically. If Being is, it must be unlimited in extent, for any limit would be a place where Being ends and non-being begins, and there is no non-being. Being is eternal in time and infinite in space. Being is one. Motion is impossible because to move is to change, and to change is for something to come to be that previously was not, which is contradiction.

The Eleatic school left the tradition with a problem that could not be ignored. If reason demands that Being is one and unchanging, and senses report that there are many things and they change, which do we trust? If we trust reason, the world we live in is illusion. If we trust the senses, then reason is somehow corrupt at the root. Every philosopher who came after had to answer this. Plato answered with the Forms. Aristotle answered with the distinction between potency and act. The pluralists answered first.

## The Many Saved

Empedocles of Acragas, in Sicily, took the Parmenidean challenge with both hands. He accepted that nothing comes from what is not and nothing passes into what is not. But he saw a way to save the world we see. There is no coming-to-be and no passing-away in the strict sense, he said. What we call generation is the mixing of what already is. What we call destruction is the separation of what continues to be. The fundamental ingredients of the world are not created and not destroyed. They merely combine and divide.

He named four such ingredients. Fire, air, water, earth. The four roots, as he called them. He gave them divine names to mark that they are eternal. Beneath every visible thing lie these four, in some proportion. Bone is a certain ratio of fire and water and earth. Blood is another ratio. Plants are another. The variety of the world is the variety of mixture.

But what mixes them, and what divides them? Here Empedocles introduces his two cosmic powers. Love unifies. Strife separates. The history of the cosmos is the alternation of these two forces. When Love prevails completely, the roots are blended in a perfect sphere, the moment of total unity. When Strife enters, the sphere breaks apart, the elements separate, the world of distinct things comes into being. When Strife prevails completely, everything is sundered into its kinds. Then Love begins to act again. The cycle resumes.

This is the first dynamic cosmology in Western thought. It is metaphysics with a tempo. Reality has phases. The world we inhabit is not a static structure but a moment in a cycle that exceeds it on both sides. Love and Strife are not gods watching from outside but forces immanent in the very being of the elements, pulling them together and pulling them apart.

Empedocles also held the Pythagorean teaching on the migration of souls. He remembered, he said, having been a boy and a girl, a bush and a bird, and a leaping journeying fish. The soul is not the body. The soul passes through bodies. He wrote a poem of purifications, instructions on how to live so that the soul might escape the wheel of birth. Bean and laurel were forbidden. Bloodshed was forbidden. Empedocles is both a natural philosopher and a religious teacher, and he did not see these roles as in tension. To know the cosmos is to know the soul's place in it, and to live rightly is to act in accordance with that knowledge.

Anaxagoras of Clazomenae took a different route through the same problem. He saw, like Empedocles, that one could not have things coming from nothing. But he refused to limit the basic ingredients to four. In everything, he said, there is a portion of everything. This is the great Anaxagorean fragment. A piece of bread, when eaten, becomes flesh and hair and bone and blood. If the bread becomes these, the bread must already contain these. Every substance contains, in some proportion, the seeds of every other. The world is not made of four elements but of infinitely many, mixed in infinitely various ratios.

What then accounts for the order of the world? Empedocles had Love and Strife. Anaxagoras had Mind. All things, he said, were together, infinite in number, infinite in smallness, and Mind arranged them. Nous, he called it. The Greek word for mind and intellect together. Mind is the finest and purest of all things. It is alone by itself, mixed with nothing, knowing all things, possessing the greatest power. Mind set in motion what was at rest, and the motion separated like from like, and the cosmos came to be.

This is the first time in Greek philosophy that a non-physical principle is explicitly invoked to explain the order of the physical world. Mind is not a body. Mind is not in the mixture. Mind acts on the mixture from outside, or rather from a different mode of being. Plato will fault Anaxagoras for not making enough use of Nous in his explanations, for falling back into mechanical accounts once the initial motion has been given. But the introduction of Mind as a principle is decisive. After Anaxagoras, the cosmos has not only a structure but an orderer, and the orderer is intelligence.

## What Was Given

Fairbanks closes his collection here, with Anaxagoras and the early reach of intelligence into nature. What we have to work with is broken. Whole books are lost. We know Heraclitus through sentences saved by writers who often misunderstood him, Parmenides through quotations, Empedocles through stitched-together remains. Yet through the broken material the shape of an event is visible.

The event is this. In something like two centuries, between the time Thales asked what everything is made of and the time Anaxagoras said that Mind has ordered all things, a way of asking was invented that had not existed before. The Greek philosophers did not have a method. They had no laboratory. They had no academy until late in the period. They had observation, conversation, argument, and the conviction that the world is the kind of thing about which truths can be stated.

What they gave us was not so much answers as the structure of all subsequent questioning. They asked what the world is made of, and the question opened the inquiry that becomes physics and chemistry. They asked what change is, and the question opened the inquiry that becomes ontology. They asked whether the senses can be trusted, and the question opened the inquiry that becomes epistemology. They asked whether the world has a guiding intelligence, and the question opened the inquiry that becomes theology and metaphysics. They asked what the soul is, and the question opened the inquiry that becomes psychology and ethics and the entire interior life of the West.

The reader who comes to Fairbanks expecting a system will be disappointed. There is no system. There are first attempts. The first attempts are arresting precisely because they are first. The questions are visible in them in a way that they are no longer visible in mature systems, where the answers have hardened over the questions and concealed them.

What the Presocratics most have to teach is the courage to begin. They did not have anyone to imitate. They invented the form of the inquiry as they conducted it. When we read them now, twenty-five centuries on, we are reading not the founders of a discipline but the first men who looked at the world without a story to lean on and tried to say what is. That moment, recovered from the broken texts, is what philosophy continues to be when it is alive. It is the willingness to ask the question again, in the open, with no answer yet. To step into the river that is not the same river, beside the fire that always burns and is always going out, beneath the Mind that has not yet finished arranging what was once altogether.

The first philosophers were the first because someone had to be. They were the first because, in a world that had been explained by myth, they refused the explanation and stood inside the question. Everyone since has been their student. To read them now is to be reminded that the question is older than any answer, and that the work of philosophy is not the possession of conclusions but the keeping-open of what they first opened.
