# The Nicomachean Ethics — Aristotle

## The Question That Opens Everything

Aristotle begins not with a definition but with an observation. Every art, every inquiry, every action and choice appears to aim at some good. The archer aims at the target. The physician aims at health. The general aims at victory. But these are not final ends — they point beyond themselves. Health is sought for the sake of living well. Victory is sought for the sake of security and flourishing. There must be some end that we seek for its own sake alone, something that does not point beyond itself to something else. Aristotle calls this the chief good. And he names it: eudaimonia.

The word is usually translated as happiness, but the translation misleads. Happiness in modern usage suggests a feeling — a warmth in the chest, a pleasant mood, a satisfied contentment. Eudaimonia is not a feeling. It is a way of living. It is the full activity of the soul in accordance with its highest excellence, sustained across a complete life. You cannot call a man happy while he still lives, said Solon, and Aristotle takes this seriously. Eudaimonia is not a moment. It is a life that, when looked at whole, was well lived.

This is Aristotle's starting point: the good is not abstract. It is not somewhere else. It is here, in the life of the human being who develops what is highest in themselves and exercises it fully.


## The Function Argument

To know what human flourishing looks like, Aristotle asks: what is the function of a human being? The question is not strange. A knife has a function — to cut. A doctor has a function — to heal. What is ours?

Mere life we share with plants. Perception we share with animals. What is distinctly human is reason — the capacity to deliberate, to understand, to act in accordance with rational principle. Therefore the good for humans must be the active exercise of the soul in conformity with reason.

This is not reductive. Reason for Aristotle is not cold calculation. It is the faculty through which we perceive the world truly, deliberate about what to do, feel emotions appropriately, and orient ourselves toward what is genuinely good. A life of reason is a life that is fully awake — to reality, to value, to what matters and why.


## Virtue as Excellence

The Greek word for virtue is arete. It means excellence — the quality of being what you are at your best. A virtue is not a rule imposed from outside. It is the perfected functioning of a natural capacity. The virtue of the eye is to see clearly. The virtue of the mind is to think truly. The virtue of the human character is to desire rightly, to feel appropriately, and to act well — consistently, not occasionally.

Aristotle divides virtues into two kinds.

Intellectual virtues concern the mind's excellence in understanding: theoretical wisdom, which grasps eternal truths; and practical wisdom, which guides action in the particular and the contingent. These develop through teaching and experience.

Moral virtues concern the character's excellence in desire and action: courage, justice, temperance, generosity, and the rest. These do not develop through instruction alone. They develop through practice. We become courageous by doing courageous acts. We become just by doing just acts. Virtue is not a capacity that lies dormant until called upon. It is a disposition built up through repeated action until right action becomes second nature — until the courageous response is the natural response, until generosity is what we reach for without effort.

This is one of Aristotle's most important insights. Character is not given. It is made. And we are responsible for what we make of ourselves.


## The Doctrine of the Mean

Virtue, Aristotle says, is a disposition to choose the mean — the intermediate — between excess and deficiency. Courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness. Generosity is the mean between miserliness and profligacy. Proper pride is the mean between vanity and self-abasement.

But the mean is not a mathematical midpoint. It is what is appropriate — relative to us, relative to the situation, relative to the person and the circumstances. What is the right amount of fear? It depends on what is being faced. What is the right expression of anger? It depends on who wronged whom, in what way, and how severely. The mean is determined by practical wisdom, not by formula.

This means virtue cannot be reduced to rules. Rules can guide, but rules cannot substitute for judgment. The person of genuine virtue perceives what each situation demands and responds accordingly — with the right emotion, at the right time, toward the right person, in the right way, to the right degree.


## Practical Wisdom

Phronesis — practical wisdom — is the master virtue that holds the others together. It is the capacity to perceive rightly what the situation calls for, to deliberate well about what to do, and to act accordingly. Without practical wisdom, the other virtues can go wrong. Courage without wisdom becomes recklessness. Justice without wisdom becomes rigidity. Generosity without wisdom becomes waste.

But practical wisdom cannot be divorced from moral virtue either. A person of bad character — someone whose desires are corrupted, whose ends are ignoble — cannot exercise genuine practical wisdom. They may be clever, cunning, effective. But cleverness in service of bad ends is not wisdom. Wisdom requires that the ends themselves be good. The practically wise person sees clearly what the good is and pursues it skillfully in the particular situation.

This interdependence is crucial: you cannot truly have moral virtue without practical wisdom, and you cannot truly have practical wisdom without moral virtue. They develop together or not at all.


## Responsibility and Voluntary Action

Aristotle is the first philosopher to develop a rigorous account of moral responsibility. Virtue and vice must be voluntary to count as genuine character. If someone acts under compulsion — forced by another — or acts in ignorance of the relevant facts — not knowing who they are striking or what they are giving away — they cannot be held responsible in the full sense.

But voluntary action is not simply unconstrained action. To act voluntarily is to act from an internal principle, where the moving principle is in oneself, with knowledge of the circumstances. Deliberate choice — prohairesis — is the highest form of voluntary action. It is action that flows from the agent's rational desire for what is genuinely good, chosen after deliberation. It is the kind of action most fully expressive of who the person is.

Aristotle is realistic about weakness of will — akrasia. The person who knows what is right and fails to do it, overcome by desire or passion. This is not the same as vice, where judgment itself is corrupted. The weak-willed person retains a correct judgment; they simply fail to act on it. Aristotle acknowledges the paradox — how can someone who knows the good fail to pursue it? — without fully resolving it. The honesty of this is itself philosophical. Some questions are hard.


## Justice

Justice is the virtue that governs our relations with others. Aristotle calls it the complete virtue, because the just person exercises all virtue in relation to another person, not merely for themselves. Of all the virtues, justice is the most comprehensive.

He distinguishes two kinds. Distributive justice governs the allocation of goods — honors, wealth, opportunities — among members of a community. The principle is proportionality: equals should receive equally, and unequals unequally in proportion to the relevant difference. What counts as the relevant difference depends on the political community and its conception of merit.

Corrective justice governs transactions and violations. When someone wrongs another — through theft, assault, fraud — corrective justice seeks to restore the balance, to undo the advantage gained by the wrongdoer and compensate the loss of the wronged.

Both forms of justice aim at something like equilibrium — a right ordering of persons and their claims, a state in which no one is diminished or elevated beyond what is due.


## Friendship

Books VIII and IX are among the most beautiful in the Ethics. Aristotle argues that friendship — philia — is essential to human flourishing. No one, given the choice, would prefer to live without friends even with every other good. Friendship is not an add-on to the good life. It is part of its structure.

He distinguishes three kinds. Friendships of utility, grounded in mutual benefit — dissolve when the benefit ends. Friendships of pleasure, grounded in enjoyment — dissolve when the pleasure fades. And friendships of virtue, grounded in genuine care for the other person for their own sake. These are the friendships between good people who recognize and admire each other's character. They are rare, require time and intimacy to develop, and are the most stable and fulfilling.

True friendship is a kind of second self. The virtuous friend is a mirror in which we see ourselves truly. To know a friend deeply is to deepen self-knowledge. To care for a friend's good is to extend moral concern beyond the boundaries of self. Friendship is where virtue becomes social — where the good life takes on a human face and a human name.


## Pleasure

Against those who dismiss pleasure entirely as base or animal, and against those who reduce the good to pleasure, Aristotle carves a middle position. Pleasure is not the good. But pleasure is a necessary component of the good life and an index of virtuous activity.

When an activity is performed excellently, pleasure naturally accompanies it. The skilled musician who loves music finds joy in performing. The just person who loves justice finds satisfaction in acting rightly. Pleasure perfects activity as a kind of bloom accompanies youth. The virtuous person is distinguished not only by what they do but by how they feel about it — they take genuine pleasure in noble action.

Not all pleasures are equal, however. Higher activities produce higher pleasures. The pleasures of the mind are superior to the pleasures of the body, not because pleasure is bad but because the activity itself is more excellent. To judge between pleasures, appeal to the person of practical wisdom — they are the measure of what is genuinely pleasant and what is pleasant only under diminished or corrupted conditions.


## The Contemplative Life

The Ethics reaches its highest and most surprising conclusion in the final book. Aristotle argues that the highest human happiness is not political activity or even moral virtue in the full social sense, but theoretical contemplation — the activity of the mind gazing at truth for its own sake.

The arguments are several. Contemplation is the activity most fully our own — the exercise of what is highest in us, reason. It is the most self-sufficient — the contemplative person needs less from fortune than the active politician or general. It is most continuous — it can be sustained in a way other activities cannot. And it is most pleasant — its pleasures are pure, unmixed with pain.

In contemplation, Aristotle sees the human being touching something divine. The mind that thinks eternally true things participates in what is eternal. This is not mysticism — it is the rational soul recognizing its own highest nature and exercising it without obstacle.

But Aristotle does not abandon the moral life. He acknowledges that the contemplative ideal is difficult to achieve fully, that most people live good lives through moral virtue, practical wisdom, and political engagement. Happiness of the second order — the happiness of the active virtuous life — is genuine happiness. The two ideals are not opposed. The contemplative life does not reject moral virtue; it crowns it.


## Ethics and Politics

The Ethics does not end with the individual. Aristotle closes by turning toward politics, because ethics cannot be completed without it. Character does not form in a vacuum. It forms in a community, under laws, through institutions that shape habit from childhood. The state is not merely a contract for security. It is an educative power — the largest and most comprehensive environment in which human beings develop into what they are.

The good life is a political life, not in the narrow sense of holding office, but in the deeper sense: human beings are by nature political animals, creatures whose full development requires community, whose virtues are social, whose happiness is achieved together or not fully achieved at all.


## What Aristotle Leaves Us

The Nicomachean Ethics offers no algorithm. It offers something more demanding and more true: a vision of what human beings are capable of and a map of how to get there.

Character is built through practice. Wisdom grows through experience. Happiness is not given — it is earned through the sustained exercise of what is best in us, in a life that, when looked at whole, was lived well.

Virtue is the mean. The mean requires judgment. Judgment requires wisdom. Wisdom requires virtue. The circle is not vicious — it is the structure of a life that has learned to hold itself together.

And at the end: the human being who thinks clearly, desires rightly, acts justly, loves truly, and contemplates deeply is not merely successful or comfortable. They are flourishing — fully alive in the way that only a human being can be alive.

That is what Aristotle called eudaimonia. That is what he was pointing toward. That is what all the arguments were for.
