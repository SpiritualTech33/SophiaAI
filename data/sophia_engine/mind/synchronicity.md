# Synchronicity

## What Synchronicity Is

Have you ever had a coincidence that seemed too perfect to be only that, a coincidence? Think of a moment when an event that meant nothing to anyone around you was charged with meaning for you. Perhaps it was an image you saw on the metro on your way to work that made you understand something you had not understood until that instant. Perhaps it was the line of a song that woke in you what had been sleeping. Perhaps it was a person who gave you the advice you needed to hear at exactly the right time.

I personally have experienced many synchronicities. In dreams, in people, in events. And that is precisely why I want to speak about synchronicity.

The word was coined by the Swiss psychiatrist Carl Gustav Jung in the late 1920s and developed across the rest of his life. Jung defined synchronicity as an acausal connecting principle. It is the meaningful coincidence of two or more events where something other than the probability of chance is involved. An inner state, a dream, a sudden thought, a feeling, lines up with an outer event in such a way that the two seem to belong to the same moment of meaning, even though no chain of cause and effect can be drawn between them.

The classic illustration is Jung's own. A patient was telling him a dream about a golden scarab. While she spoke, a tapping came at the closed window. Jung opened it and a scarab beetle, rare in that climate, flew into his hand. He gave it to her. The wall between her dream world and the world outside cracked open. Her treatment, which had been stuck for months, began to move. Nothing caused this beetle to appear at that hour. And yet for her, in that hour, it was the most necessary creature in the world.

This is what Jung means by acausal. Not that there is no order. The opposite. There is an order that does not pass through cause and effect, but through meaning. Two threads, the inner and the outer, weave together for a moment so that the meaning each carries is amplified by the other. The person who experiences such an event is rarely able to dismiss it. Something in them recognizes that they have been spoken to.

## Coincidence Or Destiny

Three words are often confused in this conversation. Coincidence, fate, and destiny. They are not the same.

Coincidence, in its ordinary usage, means an event without inner significance. Two things happen at the same time, and the simultaneity is treated as random. The probability theorists are not wrong when they remind us that, in a world of billions of events, surprising overlaps must happen by sheer arithmetic. Many coincidences are exactly this. They feel surprising and they are statistically dull.

Fate is the older idea that everything is already written. The threads are spun, measured, and cut by powers beyond us. There is no room to choose. What will happen will happen.

Destiny is the more subtle word. Destiny is not what must happen. Destiny is what wants to happen through us when we are aligned with what we are. It is the meeting of free will and a deeper pattern. It can be missed. It can be embraced. It is more like a calling than a sentence.

Synchronicity sits in a particular place among these three. It is not coincidence in the empty sense, because the event carries meaning that the observer cannot honestly ignore. It is not fate, because nothing was forced. It is closest to destiny. A synchronistic event is often a signal that the inner life and the outer life are momentarily resonating with the same frequency, that the person is on or near the path that belongs to them. Whether to listen is still their freedom.

The honest student of synchronicity learns to live in the tension between these readings. Not every striking moment is a sign from the cosmos. Some are arithmetic. But not every striking moment is arithmetic either. Some are doors. The discernment between the two is itself a skill, and like all skills it is learned by practice and humility.

## The Law Of Causality

Western science was built on the law of causality. Every effect has a cause. Every cause has its effect. This is the sixth Hermetic principle and the foundation of physics, medicine, engineering, and most of what we call rational thought. It works. To deny it would be to deny the bridge that holds the road we walk on.

Jung was not denying causality when he proposed synchronicity. He was completing it. He suggested that causality describes how events are connected through time and matter, but that there is also a second axis of connection that runs through meaning. He developed this idea in close dialogue with the physicist Wolfgang Pauli, one of the founders of quantum mechanics. Their twenty five year correspondence is one of the great unfinished conversations of the twentieth century. Pauli, working at the edge of the new physics, knew that classical causality had already cracked at the level of the atom. Jung, working at the edge of the psyche, saw the same crack from the other side.

Together they imagined a worldview in which causality and synchronicity are two complementary principles. Causality explains how the river flows downstream. Synchronicity explains why two leaves, dropped on opposite banks, sometimes touch in the middle. The river still obeys gravity. But the meeting of the leaves is its own kind of fact, and it points to an order that the language of force and mass alone cannot capture.

Synchronicity, then, is not a violation of natural law. It is a hint that the law we know is part of a larger law we have not yet articulated. A law in which mind and matter, inner and outer, psyche and world, are not finally as separate as the seventeenth century told us they were.

## The Way The Universe Speaks To Us

In my conclusion, and although it may sound mystical, synchronicity is the way the universe has of speaking to us and guiding us. Perhaps one is not familiar with the idea, but something within my being tells me constantly that the universe is a living organism, conscious and intelligent, with the capacity to communicate with us, only perhaps through forms that our limited human mind cannot yet comprehend.

Jung observed that synchronistic events tend to cluster around the activation of an archetype. An archetype, in his vocabulary, is a deep pattern of human meaning that lives in what he called the collective unconscious. The mother. The child. The wanderer. The shadow. The lover. The teacher. The death and the rebirth. These patterns belong to no single person. They belong to humanity as a species, the way the structure of the eye belongs to all who can see.

When a person enters a great transition, when an archetype begins to move through their life, the inner and the outer often come into resonance. Dreams intensify. Strangers appear at exactly the right hour. Books fall open at the right page. Songs return that one had not thought of in years. The metro window frames an image that explains everything. These are the moments most people remember on their deathbeds. Not the careful arithmetic of life, but the strange punctuation of meaning that ran through it.

The universe speaks in many alphabets. Numbers that repeat across a single day. Animals that cross the path at unusual times. The same word, spoken by three unconnected people in twenty four hours. A dream that names a stranger one will meet a week later. The line of a song on the radio at the exact second a question is forming in the heart. None of these are proofs in the scientific sense. All of them are messages in the experiential sense. They feel different from accident. They carry weight. They leave the receiver changed.

To listen well requires three things. The first is attention. The signal will not shout. It will whisper, and only the still mind hears it. The second is honesty. Not every coincidence is a sign, and the person who reads everything as a personal message to themselves loses both their balance and their humility. The third is response. A synchronicity that is noticed but not acted upon tends to grow quiet. The universe, like any teacher, tends to repeat itself once or twice and then move on.

There is a final point, and it is the deepest. The reason such moments are possible at all is that mind and world are not finally two things. The Hermetic teaching, the Buddhist teaching, the great mystic schools across cultures, all converge on this. Reality is one continuous fabric, and what we call inner and outer are two faces of the same cloth. Synchronicity is the moment when the cloth shows its weave.

When that happens, the proper response is not analysis. The proper response is gratitude. Something looked at us through the window of an event, and we were awake enough to see it look. That is enough. We rise, we walk, we live a little more in tune with what we are.

Carl Jung. Cosmos. God.
