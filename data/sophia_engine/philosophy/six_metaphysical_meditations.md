# Six Metaphysical Meditations - Rene Descartes

A synthesis of Descartes' first philosophy.


# The Project

In 1641 Rene Descartes published in Latin a small book called Meditationes de Prima Philosophia. The title in English is Meditations on First Philosophy, sometimes rendered Six Metaphysical Meditations. The book is short. It is built from six chapters that Descartes calls meditations, each meant to be read on its own day, in solitude, by a reader willing to think slowly. It is dedicated to the Doctors of the Sacred Faculty of Theology in Paris, the Sorbonne, and the dedication states the purpose of the work without ornament. Descartes wishes to demonstrate two things by reason alone, with the rigor of geometry. The first is that God exists. The second is that the human mind is really distinct from the body, and therefore that the soul does not perish with the body.

The two questions are old. What Descartes does that is new is the method. He proposes to settle them not by appeal to authority, not by appeal to Scripture, not by appeal to Aristotle or to the schools, but by a single chain of reasoning starting from a position of total doubt. He will doubt everything that can be doubted. He will reach a point where doubt is no longer possible. From that point he will build, step by step, the certainty of God and of the mind, and through them the certainty of the material world.

The Meditations are written in the first person and in the present tense. The reader is not asked to receive Descartes' results but to perform Descartes' inquiry. Each meditation is an exercise. The student sits down to think for himself. The doubts are doubts he must really feel. The discoveries are discoveries he must really make. The book is a tool for transforming the mind that uses it.

This essay synthesizes the doctrine of the six meditations in order. It tracks the argument as Descartes lays it down and presents the conclusions as conclusions of a single coherent system.


# Meditation One: The Method of Doubt

The first meditation is the demolition of the old building. Descartes begins by noticing that he has accepted many opinions in the course of his life, that some of them have turned out to be false, and that the rest are therefore not certain. Rather than examine every opinion separately, which would take an infinite time, he resolves to attack the foundations on which the opinions rest. If a foundation falls, everything built on it falls with it. He will look only at the foundations.

The first foundation he examines is the senses. He notes that the senses have deceived him in the past. A tower seen from far away looks round and turns out to be square. A stick in water looks bent. From the fact that the senses deceive sometimes, Descartes concludes that they cannot be trusted absolutely. He immediately recognizes an objection. There are sensory experiences so vivid and so close that doubting them seems absurd. He sits by the fire in a dressing gown holding a piece of paper. Can he doubt that he sits by the fire?

To this Descartes responds with the dream argument. He has often dreamed that he was sitting by a fire and was instead lying naked in bed. The sensations of dreams can be as vivid as the sensations of waking life. There is no certain mark by which waking can be distinguished from sleeping. The vividness of the experience proves nothing. The fire, the gown, the paper, may all be parts of a dream.

The dream argument allows Descartes to doubt the existence of particular things while leaving certain general kinds of knowledge standing. Even if everything he is now perceiving is a dream, the elements of the dream are still drawn from something. There must be color, extension, number, time, somewhere. The general categories survive the dream. The truths of mathematics and geometry, which do not depend on whether any particular triangle exists, also seem to survive. Two plus three is five whether he is awake or asleep.

To attack this last surviving certainty, Descartes introduces his most radical hypothesis. He supposes that there exists an evil genius, a being of great power and cunning, who has devoted all his effort to deceiving him. This being may have produced in him the appearance of a world, the appearance of his own body, the appearance even of mathematical truths, all of which are false. The hypothesis is not asserted as fact. It is admitted as possible. If it is possible, then nothing previously believed is beyond doubt. Even the simplest arithmetic might be a trick of the deceiver.

The first meditation ends with Descartes suspending assent to everything. He does not declare anything false. He simply refuses to call anything true until he has found something that resists the doubt. He warns himself that the old habits of belief are strong and will pull him back. He resolves to feign the opposite of what he formerly believed and to remain in the posture of universal doubt until certainty is found.


# Meditation Two: The Cogito and the Thinking Thing

The second meditation begins in a posture of crisis. Descartes finds himself in a kind of philosophical whirlpool. The doubt of the first meditation is so total that he cannot rest in any belief. He cannot stand. He cannot swim. He looks for a single point on which to set his foot.

He notes that even the evil genius hypothesis requires something. If the evil genius is deceiving him, then there is a him to be deceived. Whoever is being deceived must exist in order to be the object of deception. The very effort of doubt requires a doubter. The very effort of being deceived requires someone to deceive. From this Descartes draws his first certainty. The proposition I am, I exist, is necessarily true whenever it is uttered or conceived by the one who utters it.

This is the cogito. The famous formulation I think, therefore I am does not appear in the Meditations in this exact phrasing. It appears in the Discourse on the Method, published four years earlier. In the Meditations the argument is presented not as an inference but as an intuition. The certainty of one's own existence is not derived from a premise about thinking. It is grasped directly in the act of thinking. To think is to exist. To doubt is to exist. The very performance of the doubt confirms the existence of the doubter.

Descartes immediately asks what this I that exists is. He cannot say that he is a body, since the existence of bodies has been called into doubt. He cannot say that he is anything that depends on a body. What he can say is that he is a thing that thinks. Thinking is the one activity he cannot deny while doubting, because the denial would itself be thinking. He defines the thinking thing, the res cogitans, by enumerating its operations. It doubts. It understands. It affirms. It denies. It wills. It refuses. It also imagines and senses, though the objects of imagination and sensation may be illusory; the activities of imagining and sensing remain real as modes of thought.

This is the discovery of the soul as a substance. The soul is not blood, not breath, not fire mixed with air. The soul is whatever it is that performs the operations of thought. Its essence is to think. Its existence is given immediately to itself in the very act of thinking.

The second half of the meditation contains the example of the wax. Descartes takes a piece of beeswax fresh from the comb. It has its taste, its smell, its color, its shape, its hardness, its temperature. He places it near the fire. The taste vanishes. The smell evaporates. The color changes. The shape melts. The hardness becomes softness. Yet he does not doubt that the same wax remains. The sensible qualities have all changed. The wax persists. What then is the wax?

It cannot be its sensible qualities, because they have all changed. It must be something underneath the qualities that takes successively the forms of being hard or soft, cold or hot, scented or odorless, shaped one way or another. Descartes identifies this something as extension, flexibility, mutability. The wax is something extended that can take indefinitely many shapes and indefinitely many states. This is not given by the senses. The senses give only the particular shape, the particular state. The grasping of the wax as a single thing through all its variations is the work of the mind, not the senses. The wax is known by intellect, not by sight or by touch.

Descartes draws the conclusion he wants. If even a piece of wax, which seems the most ordinary of physical objects, is known not by sense but by mind, then the mind has the priority. To know any body is already to engage the mind. The existence and operation of the mind are the precondition of any other knowledge. The mind is more easily known than the body. The mind is in fact better known than anything else, because every other thing is known only through it.


# Meditation Three: The Existence of God

The third meditation moves from the certainty of the self to the certainty of God. The bridge between them is built from the analysis of ideas.

Descartes begins by formulating a general rule. Whatever he perceives clearly and distinctly is true. The cogito has this character. He sees clearly and distinctly that he exists when he thinks, and this perception cannot be doubted. He generalizes. The rule of clarity and distinctness becomes the criterion of truth. Anything else he can perceive with the same kind of certainty he will be entitled to affirm.

There remains, however, a problem. The evil genius hypothesis has not been refuted. If a deceiver exists, perhaps he has arranged matters so that even what seems clear and distinct is false. To remove this final doubt, Descartes must show that no such deceiver presides over the mind. He must show that the source of his existence is not a deceiver. To do this he must show that God exists and that God is good.

The argument proceeds through the analysis of ideas. Descartes notes that his ideas vary in their content. Some ideas seem to come from outside himself. He calls these adventitious. Some ideas seem to be invented by his own mind. He calls these factitious. Some ideas seem to belong to him by nature, neither arriving from outside nor invented by himself. He calls these innate. Among the innate ideas is the idea of God.

Descartes then introduces a distinction crucial to the argument. An idea has two kinds of reality. It has formal reality, which is its existence as a mental event, the existence of the idea itself. It has objective reality, which is the existence of its content, the thing the idea represents, considered as represented. The idea of a stone has more objective reality than the idea of a color, because a stone is a substance and a color is only a quality of a substance. The idea of an infinite being has more objective reality than the idea of a finite being.

Descartes appeals to a causal principle. Whatever is in the effect must be in the cause. The cause must have at least as much reality as the effect. This principle, traditional in scholastic philosophy, Descartes treats as self-evident. He applies it to the case of ideas. An idea, considered as a mental event, has only the formal reality of a thought, which is small. But considered with respect to its objective reality, it requires a cause adequate to the content it represents. The idea of a substance must have as its cause something that has at least the reality of a substance. The idea of an infinite substance must have as its cause something that has at least the reality of an infinite substance.

Descartes then examines the idea of God within himself. He finds in himself an idea of a being that is infinite, omniscient, omnipotent, perfectly good, the creator of all things. The objective reality of this idea is infinite. The cause of this idea must therefore be itself infinite. Descartes himself is finite. He cannot be the cause of the idea of the infinite, because he does not contain in himself the reality the idea requires. Some other being, infinite, must be the cause of the idea. That being is God.

Descartes presents a second proof in the same meditation. He asks not what causes the idea of God within him, but what causes him, the finite thinking being who has the idea. He cannot be the cause of himself, because if he had given himself existence he would also have given himself the perfections he conceives but does not possess. He has not given himself those perfections. Therefore he is not self-caused. He depends on something else for his existence. His parents gave him his body but not his mind. His existence at this moment is not given by anything in his past. Every moment that he continues to exist requires a continuing cause. Descartes argues that conservation of being is equivalent to creation of being. The being that conserves him from moment to moment must be itself underived, eternal, infinite. It must be God.

The conclusion of the third meditation is that God exists. Descartes adds one further argument. Since God is perfect, deception is incompatible with God's nature. Deception is a defect, and a perfect being has no defects. God therefore is not a deceiver. Whatever clear and distinct ideas Descartes has, their source is God, who would not give him faculties systematically designed to mislead him.

This conclusion lifts the threat of the evil genius. The genius was a hypothesis admitted as possible in the first meditation. He is now ruled out as incompatible with the existence of a good God. The rule of clarity and distinctness is therefore secured. What is clearly and distinctly perceived can now be trusted, because the faculty that perceives clearly and distinctly comes from a non-deceiving creator.


# Meditation Four: Truth and Error

A new problem arises. If God exists and is not a deceiver, why does error occur at all? Descartes errs constantly. He believes things that turn out to be false. He misjudges. He miscalculates. If his faculties come from God and God is no deceiver, the existence of error in him seems to accuse God of imperfection.

The fourth meditation is the theodicy of judgment. Descartes locates error not in any faculty considered alone, but in the interaction of two faculties. The intellect, the faculty of perceiving, is finite. It does not perceive everything. There are matters about which it perceives clearly and distinctly. There are other matters about which it perceives only confusedly or not at all. The will, the faculty of judging and choosing, is in a certain sense infinite. The will has no fixed limit. It can affirm or deny anything that the intellect presents to it, including things the intellect has not perceived clearly.

Error occurs when the will affirms or denies what the intellect has not clearly perceived. The will reaches beyond the bounds of the intellect's certain grasp. The will pronounces judgment on what is still obscure. The judgment may happen to be correct by chance, but it is not knowledge. When the judgment is incorrect, the result is error. The fault is not in God. God gave both the intellect and the will. The intellect is finite, as is appropriate to a creature. The will is free, as is appropriate to a being made in the image of God. The defect lies in the use of these faculties. When the will is restrained to operate only within the bounds of clear perception, error does not occur. When the will is extended beyond the bounds of clear perception, error becomes possible.

Descartes defines error as a privation rather than a positive being. Error is not a thing produced by God. Error is the absence of a knowledge that should have been present, an absence produced by the misuse of freedom. The whole structure of finite intelligence requires that the intellect be limited and the will free; this is the cost of having creatures who are genuinely free. God's perfection is compatible with the existence of creatures whose freedom permits them to err, because the existence of such creatures is itself a positive perfection in the order of creation.

Descartes also clarifies what freedom is. The highest freedom is not indifference between alternatives. Indifference is the lowest grade of freedom, the kind we have when we lack the knowledge that would incline us toward one choice or the other. The highest freedom occurs when the intellect sees the truth so clearly that the will spontaneously inclines toward it. To be inclined by the clear is to be more free, not less, because it is to act according to one's own deepest nature as a knowing being. The free act and the right act, when knowledge is full, coincide.

The practical lesson of the fourth meditation is the discipline of judgment. The seeker after truth must withhold assent from anything the intellect does not clearly and distinctly perceive. The will must be trained to wait. Where the perception is clear, the will may affirm without danger. Where the perception is obscure, the will must suspend judgment. This discipline is not the loss of freedom but its highest exercise. To follow the clear is to follow the good. To affirm beyond the clear is to misuse the gift of choice.


# Meditation Five: The Essence of Matter and the Ontological Argument

The fifth meditation turns from the self to the question of what material things are, considered apart from whether they actually exist. Descartes finds within himself ideas of extended things. He examines these ideas to see what their content is. He finds extension in length, breadth, and depth. He finds figure, position, motion, and duration.

He then notes something striking. When he examines the idea of a triangle, he finds in it properties that he did not put there. The triangle has three sides. The sum of its angles equals two right angles. The greater side subtends the greater angle. These properties are not invented by Descartes. They are discovered. They were already there in the idea of the triangle. They belong to its essence. They are true whether or not any actual triangle exists in the world. They are independent of his will.

Descartes calls these true and immutable natures. They are not creatures of the mind. They are not fictions. They are structures the mind discovers when it attends to the contents of its own ideas. Geometry is the science of such natures applied to extended substance. The certainty of geometry rests on the certainty of these natures.

This analysis prepares the ontological argument for God's existence. Descartes asks whether existence belongs to the essence of God in the same way that the equality of the angles to two right angles belongs to the essence of the triangle. He answers that it does. God is conceived as a supremely perfect being. Existence is a perfection. To conceive of a supremely perfect being as not existing is to conceive of a supremely perfect being that lacks a perfection, which is a contradiction. Therefore existence belongs to the essence of God. Therefore God exists.

The ontological argument is independent of the causal arguments of the third meditation. The causal arguments asked what could have produced the idea of God in a finite mind, or the existence of a finite mind itself. The ontological argument asks only what is contained in the idea of God when the idea is examined attentively. The conclusion is the same. God exists. The two routes meet.

Descartes notes one further consequence. The certainty of clear and distinct perception itself depends on God. Even mathematical truths, while they appear self-evident when attended to directly, can become uncertain when the mind looks away. The memory of having proved something is not the same as the present clarity of proving it. Without God, the certainty of past insights would be in question at every moment when they are not actively being thought. With God, the certainty of clear and distinct perception is established once and for all, because God guarantees that what is clearly and distinctly perceived is true.

The objection sometimes raised against this part of the system is known as the Cartesian Circle. The objection is that Descartes uses clear and distinct perception to prove God, and then uses God to validate clear and distinct perception. Descartes' response, given in the replies to objections appended to the Meditations, is that the clarity of the cogito and the clarity of the proofs of God do not themselves require divine guarantee in the moment of being grasped. The guarantee is needed only for the durable, lifelong reliability of the criterion, not for its momentary force. Whether this answer fully resolves the difficulty has been debated in the centuries since. Descartes himself considered the matter settled.


# Meditation Six: The Existence of Material Things and the Real Distinction

The sixth and final meditation completes the system. It establishes the existence of material things, the real distinction between mind and body, and the special character of the mind's union with its own body.

Descartes begins by noting that material things can at least exist, since God has the power to create whatever Descartes can clearly and distinctly conceive. He has clear and distinct ideas of extended things. Therefore extended things can exist. The question is whether they actually do.

The argument for actual existence proceeds through the analysis of sensation. Descartes has sensations that present themselves to him as coming from outside, that arrive without his will, sometimes against his will. He cannot turn them off by deciding to. They have an active source somewhere. The source is either God himself, or an active substance other than himself in which the cause of the sensations is contained. If the source were God acting directly, while presenting these sensations as if they came from extended bodies, God would be a deceiver. But God is not a deceiver. Therefore the sensations come from real extended bodies. The material world exists.

Descartes then establishes the real distinction between mind and body. The argument turns on what can be clearly and distinctly conceived. He can clearly and distinctly conceive of himself as a thinking thing without any extension. He can clearly and distinctly conceive of bodies as extended things without any thought. Two things that can each be clearly and distinctly conceived without the other are really distinct, because whatever can be so conceived can be so created by God. Therefore mind and body are really distinct substances. The mind is thinking substance. The body is extended substance. Neither requires the other for its existence.

This real distinction is what supports the immortality of the soul, since the soul does not depend on the body for its being. It also raises a problem. How does the mind, which is unextended, relate to the body, which is extended? Descartes does not pretend the problem is easy. He approaches it carefully.

He distinguishes two kinds of relation a mind might have to a body. A sailor is in his ship, but the sailor's relation to the ship is external. If the ship is damaged, the sailor perceives the damage by inspection, not by feeling. He sees the leak; he does not feel the leak. If the mind were related to the body in this way, it would perceive the body's states only intellectually. Damage to the body would be observed but not felt.

This is not how the mind relates to the body. When the body is damaged, the mind feels pain. When the body needs food, the mind feels hunger. The mind is not in the body as a sailor in a ship. The mind and the body together form a single unified being. The union is intimate. The sensations of pain, hunger, thirst, and pleasure are not intellectual judgments about the body. They are direct experiences in which the mind is informed of the state of the body in a way no observer outside the body could be informed.

This union, while real, is not a third kind of substance. The mind remains mind. The body remains body. The union is a fact about the way these two distinct substances are joined in the case of a human being. Descartes acknowledges that the manner of the union is difficult to understand. The senses are reliable for the purposes for which they were given, namely the preservation of the body and the guidance of action in the world. They are not reliable as a guide to the metaphysical nature of things, which is reached only by the intellect.

Descartes also describes how the body communicates with the mind. The nerves carry signals from the parts of the body to the brain. A given signal in the brain produces a given sensation in the mind. The system is generally reliable but can be misled. When a foot is injured, the same kind of signal can be produced by an injury further up in the nerve. The mind feels pain in the foot even when the cause is in the leg. The system is calibrated for the most common cases. The occasional error in interpretation is the price of having a reliable everyday sensitivity. This explains the phenomenon of phantom limb pain, which Descartes himself notes as an example.

Finally, Descartes returns to the doubts of the first meditation and dissolves them. The dream argument is answered through the criterion of coherence. Dreams do not connect with the rest of life in the way waking experience does. Waking experience is continuous with memory, with the reports of others, with the use of reason. Dreams are interrupted, isolated, and disconnected. When the senses, memory, and intellect cohere, the experience is waking. When they fail to cohere, the experience is dream. The systematic coherence of waking life, grounded in the reliability that a non-deceiving God provides, distinguishes truth from illusion in the ordinary affairs of life.


# The Architecture as a Whole

The six meditations form a single argument. The first meditation tears down. The second establishes the existence of the self as a thinking thing. The third establishes the existence of God by causal reasoning from the idea of the infinite. The fourth explains how error is possible without impeaching the goodness of God. The fifth establishes the essence of matter as extension and proves God's existence again by the ontological argument. The sixth establishes the existence of material things, the real distinction between mind and body, and the intimate union of the mind with its own body.

The order is not accidental. Each step depends on what comes before. The self comes first, because doubt cannot reach behind the existence of the doubter. God comes next, because without God the criterion of clarity has no permanent guarantor. The rules for the use of judgment come third, because without them the criterion cannot be applied without error. The essence of matter comes fourth, because the analysis of ideas reveals what bodies are before the question of their existence can be raised. The existence of bodies comes last, because it depends on a non-deceiver who would not give us sensations of bodies that did not exist.

The system rests on three substances. There is thinking substance, the mind, whose essence is thought. There is extended substance, body, whose essence is extension. There is infinite substance, God, who is the ultimate ground and creator of the other two. Finite minds and finite bodies depend on God for their continuing existence. The union of mind and body in a human being is a particular intimate joining of two finite substances under the conserving power of the infinite substance.

The method of the Meditations is the method of clear and distinct perception, applied with the discipline of withholding judgment whenever clarity fails. The instrument is the mind itself, freed from the prejudices of childhood and the deceptions of the senses, returning to itself to ask what it can know by its own light. The achievement is a metaphysics that begins from the inside, from the bare fact of thinking, and reaches outward through God to the recovery of the world.

The Meditations established the framework within which much of modern philosophy unfolded. The questions Descartes raised about the relation of mind and body, the criterion of certainty, the role of God in grounding knowledge, the priority of the first person, and the place of doubt in the search for truth, became the central questions of the philosophical tradition that followed him. The answers he proposed have been accepted, rejected, refined, and reformulated for nearly four centuries. The book remains. It can still be read on six consecutive days, in solitude, by anyone willing to think slowly. The exercise is the same.
