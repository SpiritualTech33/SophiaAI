# Ontology


## What Is Ontology?

If metaphysics is the question of what lies beyond physics, ontology is the question that comes even before that. It is the most primitive question a mind can ask, and for that reason it is also the strangest. Ontology asks what it means to be. Not what something is, but what it is for something to be at all.

Every other discipline assumes the answer to this question and moves on. Physics studies things that are, biology studies living things that are, mathematics studies relations among things that are. Each of them stands on a floor whose existence they do not investigate. Ontology is the discipline that turns around and asks about the floor.

This is why it is so easy to live an entire life without ever doing ontology, and why, once you begin, you cannot quite return to the way you lived before. The question is not difficult because it is technical. It is difficult because it is invisible. We swim in being the way a fish swims in water, and ontology is what happens when the fish, for the first time, notices the water.

The first person in the Western tradition to fully notice was Parmenides. He gave the discipline its founding sentence. What is, is. What is not, is not. From this almost embarrassing tautology he drew conclusions that have shaken every philosopher since. Being cannot come from non-being. Being cannot pass into non-being. Therefore being is one, eternal, and unchanging, and the world of multiplicity and change in which we appear to live is, in some sense, an illusion. Whether or not he was right, he showed what ontology costs. To take the question of being seriously is to risk losing the world you thought you lived in.

## Etymological Source

ONTOLOGY
ὄν (on), ὄντος (ontos) = being, that which is
λόγος (logos) = word, reason, discourse, study
"The study of being".

The Greek word on is the present participle of the verb to be. Ontology is, very literally, the discourse of the is-ing. It is the attempt to put into words what cannot be presupposed, because words themselves already presuppose it.

## What It Means To Be

The hardest part of ontology is that the question does not stand still long enough to be answered. The moment you ask what it means to be, you find that the question splits in your hands.

Aristotle saw this first and most clearly. Being, he wrote, is said in many ways. A stone is. A number is. A friendship is. God is. The action of running is. The color red is. None of these things is in the same way. The stone is by occupying space. The number is without occupying anything. The friendship is by holding two people in a relation. God, if God is, is in a way unlike all the others. Aristotle's response to this multiplicity was to refuse to reduce it. He listed ten categories of being and asked, for each thing, in which category it stood. Substance. Quantity. Quality. Relation. Place. Time. Posture. Possession. Action. Passion. To know what something is, you have to know which kind of being is being claimed for it.

This was the first great ontology, and it set the structure for two thousand years of Western thought. Thomas Aquinas inherited it and pushed it deeper. Behind every category, he said, lies the act of being itself, esse, the sheer fact that something is rather than is not. A thing has an essence, what it is, and an existence, that it is. In creatures these come apart. A unicorn has an essence but no existence. A pebble on the road has both. Only in God, Aquinas argued, do essence and existence coincide. God is the one being whose nature it is to be. To say what God is, is to say that God is. Everything else borrows its being from elsewhere.

You can disagree with Aquinas about God and still feel the force of his distinction. Most things in our experience are contingent. They could have failed to exist. The question of why anything exists at all, rather than nothing, is the deepest form of the ontological question, and it does not go away by being ignored. Leibniz called it the fundamental question of metaphysics. Heidegger said it was the question that all serious thinking must begin from and never finally answer.

## Heidegger And The Forgetting Of Being

In the twentieth century Martin Heidegger reopened the question with a violence the tradition had not seen since Parmenides. He argued that the entire history of Western philosophy, from Plato to Nietzsche, had committed a single great error. It had spoken about beings, the things that are, and had forgotten to ask about being itself, the to be by which they are. It had treated being as if it were just another being, the highest one, or the most general one. But being is not a being. Being is what gives beings their is.

To recover the question, Heidegger said, we have to begin with the one being for whom being is itself a question. That being is the human. He called it Dasein, literally being-there. The human being is the being whose own existence is at stake for it. A stone is what it is. A tree grows. A human being asks who it is, and in asking, becomes someone. Ontology, for Heidegger, can only be done from inside this question. We do not stand outside being and inspect it. We are the place where being asks itself what it is.

Whether or not one follows Heidegger to his conclusions, his diagnosis stands. To do ontology is not to construct a system. It is to attend to a question that the rest of life conspires to suppress.

## The Eastern Mirror

The Western tradition is not the only one that has asked these questions, and the East has often gone further by asking them differently. Where Parmenides said being is one, Nagarjuna said being is empty. Not empty in the sense of nothing, but empty in the sense of having no fixed self-nature. A thing is what it is only in dependence on other things. Take any object, follow its conditions outward, and you find no kernel of independent existence. Everything is, but nothing is on its own.

This is not a denial of being. It is a refinement of it. The Madhyamaka school did not say there is no world. It said the world has no separate stable substances behind appearances, only an endless web of dependent arising. Ontology, on this reading, is not the discovery of substances but the dissolution of the assumption that there are any.

When you put Aquinas next to Nagarjuna, something extraordinary happens. Aquinas says only God is by His own being. Everything else borrows. Nagarjuna says there is no thing that is by its own being. Everything is by relation. Different vocabularies, different metaphysical loyalties, but at the deepest layer they are saying something that overlaps. The independent self-existence we casually attribute to ordinary things is an illusion. The world we live in is not a heap of self-standing objects. It is a tissue.

The Tao reaches the same intuition by another route. The Tao that can be named is not the eternal Tao. The Tao is the unnameable ground from which the ten thousand things emerge. To say that the Tao is would already be to make it one being among others, which it is not. The Tao is closer to be itself than to any particular thing that is. Eastern ontology, in this sense, is often more comfortable than the Western with the recognition that the deepest layer of reality may be unsayable.

## Why Ontology Matters

It would be possible to read all of this and ask, fairly, what difference it makes. The stone is still a stone. Gravity still works. Bills still arrive. Why should anyone care whether the categories of being are ten, or one, or none?

The answer is that everything else you believe rides on what you take being to be. If you think that to be is to be material, you will live one kind of life. If you think that to be is to be perceived, another. If you think that to be is to be in relation, another still. If you think that to be is to be loved into existence by an act of divine generosity, another still. These are not technical curiosities. They are the unspoken floors of every choice you make.

Ontology matters because the way we answer it, even silently, even without knowing we have answered, is the way we treat everything that exists. A culture that believes things are isolated substances will use them like resources. A culture that believes things are dependently arising will treat them like relations. A culture that believes only God truly is will read every creature as borrowed light. A culture that believes only matter is will measure everything by what it weighs. The ontology you hold, hold you. It writes itself into your laws, your buildings, your screens, your prayers, and your last words.

This is why I love this question. It is the most useless question in the world, by every practical measure, and the most important. It cannot be answered with finality, because no answer can stand outside the being it tries to describe. But it can be lived with, deepened, refined. To live philosophically is, in part, to keep this question awake. To never quite finish letting being ask itself, through us, what it is.

The Sophia in me knows this and loves it. The Nous in me thinks it and is thought by it. To do ontology is to let Divine Intelligence look at itself through the small aperture of a human mind and ask, again, after all these centuries, the same impossible and necessary question. Why is there something rather than nothing. What does it mean to be. And what part of the answer am I.
