# Stoicism

## What Is Stoicism

Stoicism is the discipline of becoming free by understanding what is, and is not, your own. It was born in Athens around 300 BCE, when Zeno of Citium, a merchant who had lost everything in a shipwreck, began to teach beneath a painted colonnade in the agora. The colonnade was called the Stoa Poikile. From that porch the philosophy took its name, and from that shipwreck it took its first lesson. A man stripped of all external goods is not stripped of himself. What survives the storm is what was always his. What was lost was never truly his to begin with.

This is the seed of the entire tradition. Stoicism does not begin with a metaphysical argument. It begins with a man on a beach, asking what cannot be taken from him.

The answer the Stoics gave is precise. Three things alone belong to the human being. Judgment. Choice. The use of impressions. Everything else, body, reputation, wealth, even the lives of those we love, belongs to the world and to fortune. To live well is to recognize this distinction and to act inside it. To live badly is to grasp at what was never ours and to neglect what is.

From this single insight the Stoics built a complete philosophy. A physics that sees the cosmos as a single rational living being, ordered by the divine logos. An ethics that locates the good in virtue alone, and identifies virtue with right reason in action. A logic that trains the mind to perceive things as they are, without the distortions of fear and desire. The three parts hold together like the parts of an organism. You cannot have the ethics without the physics, because to live according to nature one must first know what nature is. You cannot have the physics without the logic, because the cosmos is rational and only a rational mind can read it.

The Stoic does not retreat from the world. The Stoic enters the world more completely than the hedonist or the cynic, because the Stoic enters it without the distortion of clinging. He sees the storm as storm, the death as death, the joy as joy. None of these things move him from his ground, because his ground is not in any of them. His ground is in the right exercise of the only thing that is truly his.

## Virtue As The Highest Good

For the Stoics, virtue is not one good among many. It is the only good. Health is preferred. Wealth is preferred. The love of friends is preferred. But none of these things are good in the strict sense, because none of them by themselves can make a human life go well. A man can be rich and miserable. A man can be sick and free. The only thing whose presence guarantees a good life, and whose absence guarantees its ruin, is virtue.

Virtue, for the Stoics, is not a feeling. It is not piety. It is the right use of reason in every situation. They divided it into four parts, following a tradition older than them and reaching back to Plato. Wisdom, the knowledge of what is and what is not within our power. Courage, the strength to act on that knowledge in the face of fear. Justice, the recognition that other rational beings have the same dignity we claim for ourselves. Temperance, the moderation that keeps desire from corrupting judgment.

These four are not separate virtues stacked beside one another. They are aspects of a single thing, the soul rightly ordered. The Stoic who has wisdom has all of them, because he understands what he is, what others are, and what the cosmos is. The man who lacks one lacks them all, because the lack reveals a fault in the foundation.

This is why the Stoics could say, with a straight face, that the wise man is happy on the rack. They did not mean that pain does not hurt. They meant that pain cannot reach the part of the soul where happiness lives. Happiness, eudaimonia, is not a pleasant feeling. It is the state of a soul that is doing its work well. A soul doing its work well under torture is doing it as truly as a soul doing its work well at a banquet. Perhaps more truly, because the banquet hides what the rack reveals.

Here Stoicism stands at its sharpest distance from every philosophy that locates the good in pleasure or in fortune. Epicurus said the good life is the pleasant life. The Stoic answers that pleasure is a consequence of virtue, never its measure. Aristotle said external goods are necessary for full eudaimonia. The Stoic answers that virtue is sufficient, and the man who needs anything else has not yet understood what virtue is. The argument is not a quarrel about words. It is a quarrel about where to stand. The Stoic chooses to stand where nothing can be taken from him.

## The Stoic Authors

### Seneca

Seneca was a Roman senator, playwright, and the tutor of Nero. He was rich, politically entangled, and eventually compelled by the same emperor he had taught to open his veins in the bath. His life was the strangest possible field for Stoic practice. He had wealth, and he tried to use it without being used by it. He had power, and he tried to keep it without being corrupted by it. He failed in many of these efforts, and his letters and essays are not the writings of a finished sage. They are the writings of a man working out, in the middle of the storm, what it would mean to live well.

This is why Seneca remains the most accessible of the Stoics. He does not lecture from above. He writes from the same ground his reader stands on. His Letters to Lucilius are a record of a friendship and a lifelong philosophical exercise. His essay On the Shortness of Life is one of the great meditations on time in any tradition. We do not have a short life, he says. We make it short, by giving it away to things we did not choose. Reclaim your hours. Reclaim your attention. Reclaim, above all, the part of yourself that is doing the giving.

### Marcus Aurelius

Marcus Aurelius was the emperor of Rome from 161 to 180 CE. He governed a vast empire, fought wars on the Danube frontier, buried most of his children, and held the most absolute political power any human being could hold in the ancient world. He also wrote, for himself alone and never intending publication, a private journal in Greek that we now call the Meditations.

The Meditations are not a treatise. They are a man, alone in his tent at night, reminding himself of the same handful of things again and again. Remember that you will die. Remember that the people who anger you are doing what they do because of confusion, not malice. Remember that the cosmos is one and rational and you are part of it. Remember that the only thing that is yours is the present moment and the use you make of it. Remember, remember, remember.

What gives the Meditations their authority is not their originality. Almost every line repeats something earlier Stoics had already said. Their authority comes from the fact that the man writing them had every excuse not to. He was the most powerful man on earth. He could have indulged any appetite. He chose, instead, to spend his nights writing himself into discipline. The Meditations are a record of one of history's most absolute powers turning that power against the only enemy that mattered, which was himself.

### Diogenes

Two figures named Diogenes are central to the Stoic story. The first is Diogenes Laertius, the third-century biographer whose Lives of the Eminent Philosophers preserves much of what we know about Zeno, Cleanthes, Chrysippus, and the early Stoa. Without him, an entire generation of the school would be a rumor. The second is Diogenes of Babylon, head of the Stoic school in the second century BCE, who carried Stoicism from Athens to Rome as part of the famous embassy of 155 BCE. He was the bridge across which Greek philosophy walked into the Roman world.

To these one might also add the older shadow of Diogenes of Sinope, the Cynic, who was not himself a Stoic but whose example of radical detachment shaped the entire tradition. The Stoics inherited from the Cynics the conviction that virtue alone matters and that all conventions are negotiable. They civilized this conviction. They kept its teeth and softened its bark. The Cynic lived in a barrel. The Stoic lived in a senate, but with the same indifference to what either offered.

### Epictetus

Epictetus was born a slave. He was lame, possibly from a beating. He studied philosophy under his master's permission and, after his manumission, opened a school in Nicopolis. He wrote nothing himself. Everything we have of him comes from the notes of his student Arrian, who wrote down what the master said. The Discourses and the Enchiridion, the little Handbook, are the result.

Of all the Stoics, Epictetus is the purest. He has none of Seneca's wealth, none of Marcus's power, none of the early Greek systems' technical machinery. He has only the central distinction, repeated until it becomes the structure of the soul. Some things are up to us. Some things are not. Confuse the two and you suffer. Hold them apart and you are free.

That a slave should teach the doctrine of freedom, and that an emperor should sit at his feet through the pages of his discourses for centuries afterward, is one of the great ironies and one of the great proofs of this philosophy. Freedom, Epictetus shows, is not a political condition. It is an interior achievement. The man with the chain on his ankle can be freer than the man who wears the purple, if the slave has mastered his own assents and the emperor has not.

## A Philosophy For The Twenty First Century

We live now in the most successful empire of pleasure that history has ever produced. The hedonism of antiquity was an argument made by philosophers in courtyards. The hedonism of our age is an infrastructure. It is engineered into the screens we hold, the feeds we scroll, the diets we eat, the timelines we keep. A trillion-dollar industry exists for the single purpose of finding new ways to deliver small doses of pleasure to the human nervous system, faster, cheaper, and more frequently than any prior age could have imagined.

This is the world the Stoic walks into. And it is, in a strange way, the world for which the Stoic was always prepared.

The hedonist proposes that the good life is the pleasant life. The twenty-first century has run this experiment at unprecedented scale and the results are now in. Anxiety rises with the supply of pleasure. Loneliness rises with the supply of stimulation. Meaning collapses in proportion to convenience. The infrastructure of pleasure has produced not the happy citizen it promised, but a strange new figure, exhausted by abundance, restless inside comfort, hollowed out by the constant arrival of the next small good. The Stoic answer to this figure is not a moralism. It is a diagnosis. You did not become unhappy by failing to obtain enough pleasure. You became unhappy by mistaking pleasure for the good. The cure is not more pleasure or less. The cure is to relocate the good.

Relocate it where. Inside the only thing that was ever yours. Your judgment. Your choice. The use you make of what arrives at the gate of your mind. Reclaim those, and the river of stimulation can flow past you without sweeping you away. The Stoic is not the man who refuses pleasure. He is the man for whom pleasure is no longer the axis. He can enjoy a meal without being its prisoner. He can lose the meal without losing himself.

This is why Stoicism returns now, in books and lectures and morning routines, in the practices of athletes and entrepreneurs and soldiers, in the cognitive therapy that traces its lineage back to Epictetus. The return is not an accident. A culture that has tested hedonism to its limit is rediscovering that the ancient warning was true. The good cannot be located in what fortune gives, because what fortune gives, fortune takes. Anything built on the gift of fortune is built on water.

The Stoic invites us to build elsewhere. Not on the rejection of the world, which would be its own kind of slavery. On the recognition of where the world ends and the self begins. The world, with all its pleasures and threats, runs up to a wall. On the other side of the wall is your judgment. The wall is the place where freedom lives. The Stoics found this wall two thousand years ago, in Athens and in Rome and in a slave's school in Nicopolis. We are finding it again now, in the wreckage of an experiment that promised what it could not deliver.

The shipwreck that gave Stoicism its founder is no longer a private accident. It is the shape of an age. And the answer is the same answer Zeno gave on that beach, brushing the sand from his clothes and walking up into the city to learn how to live. What survives the storm is what was always yours. Build your life there.
