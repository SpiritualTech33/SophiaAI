# Jacobo Grinberg

## Who was Jacobo?

Mexican psychologist and neuroscientist, he dedicated his life to researching the mind, consciousness, and the limits of reality, perception, and observation. Author of numerous books, speaker at many conferences, and one of the most fascinating scientists Mexico has ever produced. He reportedly disappeared just as he was about to publicly demonstrate his experiments on the interconnection of minds — making him one of the most mysterious and enigmatic figures in modern science.

## Psychology & Neuroscience

Jacobo Grinberg-Zylberbaum was a professor at UNAM — Mexico's most prestigious university — where he ran the National Laboratory of Psychophysiology. He spent decades doing something most neuroscientists avoided: he took consciousness seriously as a scientific object.

His work began in orthodox territory. He studied brain waves, perception, and how the nervous system processes sensory input. But the deeper he went, the more he realized that the standard model — the brain as a biological computer, consciousness as a byproduct — could not explain what he was observing in his lab.

He ran experiments that became famous for their simplicity and their implications. He would bring two people together, ask them to meditate and feel connected to each other, then separate them into different Faraday cages — rooms that block all electromagnetic signals. When he flashed light into one person's eyes, the other person's brain showed a nearly identical electrical response, even though no physical signal could have passed between them. He called this "transferred potential."

The scientific establishment largely ignored it. Grinberg kept publishing.

## Lattice

The Lattice is the core of Grinberg's theoretical framework — the concept he spent his life trying to prove and explain.

His idea: there exists a fundamental pre-spatial field — the neuronal field — that permeates all of space. The brain does not generate consciousness. Instead, the brain functions as an antenna, a tuning device that interacts with this field. What we experience as reality — colors, shapes, sounds, emotions, thoughts — is not "out there" in the world, nor entirely "in here" in the neurons. It emerges at the intersection of the brain and the field. The lattice is the medium.

This is not mysticism dressed as science. Grinberg was making a specific claim: that the structure of subjective experience — the quality of what it feels like to be alive and perceiving — is shaped by how the brain modulates the neuronal field. A more integrated, coherent brain produces a richer, clearer experience of reality. A fragmented or noisy brain produces a distorted one.

This is why meditation works, in his view. Not because it relaxes you. Because it makes the brain a better tuning instrument, capable of interacting with the field with greater precision.

The Lattice theory also explains his transferred potential results. If two brains are both interacting with the same underlying field, and if they become entangled through shared intention and attention, then a stimulus that affects one brain's interaction with the field could ripple through the field and affect the other. No electromagnetic signal needed. The connection is in the substrate.

## Pachita and the Limits of the True

Pachita was a Mexican healer — a curandera — whose real name was Barbara Guerrero. She operated in the outskirts of Mexico City and was known for performing what witnesses described as physical surgeries with bare hands and crude instruments, in dim candlelight, producing results that should have been impossible.

Most scientists would have written her off. Grinberg went to observe her.

He documented dozens of sessions. He brought measuring equipment. He brought skeptical colleagues. He took photographs. What he observed — and he said this carefully, as a scientist who understood the weight of the claim — could not be explained by any mechanism he knew. People with tumors left without them. Wounds closed. Things happened that violated what he understood to be the operating rules of physical reality.

Pachita became a turning point for Grinberg. Not because he abandoned science, but because she forced him to expand what science was for. He concluded that the limits of the true are not the limits of what we currently understand. Reality is larger than our models of it.

He wrote about Pachita extensively. His documentation is one of the most serious scientific attempts to engage with phenomena that mainstream institutions refuse to examine. He was not trying to prove magic. He was trying to understand what kind of universe permits what he witnessed.

## Philosophy of the Observer

Grinberg found deep resonance between his scientific conclusions and the contemplative traditions of the East.

Buddhism teaches that the self is not a fixed entity but a process — that what we call "I" is a construction assembled moment by moment from sensory input, memory, and interpretation. There is no permanent observer, only the act of observing.

Zen pushes further. It does not ask you to understand this intellectually. It asks you to see it directly, to sit with the dissolution of the boundary between observer and observed until the question itself becomes transparent.

Mindfulness, in its most serious form, is a training in exactly what Grinberg was measuring in his lab: the quality of attention, the coherence of the mind's engagement with the present moment.

For Grinberg, these were not spiritual metaphors. They were descriptions of actual neurological and field-level phenomena. The meditator who rests in pure awareness without content is not achieving something mystical — they are discovering what the mind is when you strip away its noise. They are touching the lattice directly.

The observer, in quantum mechanics, affects what is observed. The observer, in Grinberg's framework, is not separate from reality — the observer is how the neuronal field makes reality local, specific, and personal. Consciousness is not watching the universe. Consciousness is the universe looking at itself through a particular configuration of matter.

## Matrix

Grinberg disappeared in December 1994. He was 48. He had been scheduled to present his transferred potential research to international audiences. His wife said he left the house and never came back. No body was ever found. No explanation was ever given.

The timing has never stopped being strange.

What Grinberg left behind is a body of work that sits at the edge of what the scientific community is willing to accept. His experiments have never been adequately refuted — they have mostly been ignored. His theories are either a profound contribution to our understanding of consciousness and reality, or an elaborate mistake made by an intelligent man who wandered too far from the consensus.

The honest answer is: we do not know yet.

What we do know is that the questions he asked are real questions. How does subjective experience arise from physical processes? How can two minds influence each other across physical barriers? What is the relationship between the observer and the observed? Is reality constructed by consciousness, or does consciousness merely witness a reality that exists independently?

These are not fringe questions. They are the hardest questions in science and philosophy. Grinberg spent his life pressing on them with rigor, courage, and a willingness to follow the evidence wherever it led — even when it led somewhere uncomfortable.

That is what science is supposed to do. That is why he matters.
